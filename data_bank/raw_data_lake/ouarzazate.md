# Ouarzazate Airport (OZZ)

## Overview

Ouarzazate Airport (`IATA: OZZ`, `ICAO: GMMZ`) is a regional airport serving the city of Ouarzazate and the surrounding Drâa-Tafilalet region of Morocco. It acts as a crucial gateway for tourists heading to the Sahara Desert, the stunning kasbahs like the UNESCO World Heritage site Aït Benhaddou, and the nearby Atlas Film Studios. While significantly smaller than Morocco's major international hubs, the airport is vital for the region's tourism and film production industries.

## Quick Facts

* **Official Name:** Ouarzazate Airport
* **IATA Code:** `OZZ`
* **ICAO Code:** `GMMZ`
* **Location:** Approximately 2 km (1.2 miles) northwest of Ouarzazate city center.
* **Operator:** ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority)
* **Website:** [www.onda.ma](http://www.onda.ma/en/Our-Airports/Ouarzazate-Airport)

---

## Terminals

Ouarzazate Airport features **one small, single terminal building** that handles all domestic and international arrivals and departures. The facility is modest and functional, designed to process passengers efficiently. Due to the limited number of flights, the terminal is easy to navigate and generally not crowded, offering a straightforward travel experience.

---

## Airlines and Destinations

The flight schedule at Ouarzazate is limited and can be highly seasonal. The most reliable connection is the domestic flight to Casablanca, which links the region to the global flight network.

* **Primary Operator:**
    * **Royal Air Maroc Express:** Operates the essential domestic route to Casablanca (`CMN`), allowing for connections to international flights.

* **International Operators (Often Seasonal):**
    * **Ryanair:** Has historically operated seasonal routes from Spanish cities like Barcelona (`BCN`) and Madrid (`MAD`).
    * **Transavia:** May offer seasonal flights from European hubs such as Paris-Orly (`ORY`).

**Important Note:** International flight routes to Ouarzazate can vary significantly by season and year. It is crucial to check directly with airlines for the most current schedules. Most international visitors arrive by connecting through Mohammed V Airport in Casablanca.

---

## Passenger Services and Facilities

The services at Ouarzazate Airport are basic and reflect its size.

* **Dining and Shopping:** Expect a small café or snack bar for basic refreshments and a small shop selling newspapers, essentials, and some local souvenirs. The selection is limited compared to larger airports.
* **Financial Services:** An ATM and a currency exchange desk are typically available to serve arriving passengers.
* **Car Rental:** Several local and international car rental agencies have desks at the airport. Renting a car, particularly a 4x4, is a very popular option for visitors planning to explore the desert landscapes and mountain gorges independently.
* **Wi-Fi:** Wi-Fi may be available but might not be as robust as in larger international airports.

---

## Ground Transportation

Due to its close proximity to the city, getting from the airport to your accommodation is quick and simple.

### Taxis
* **Petit Taxis** (typically red in Ouarzazate) are readily available directly outside the terminal exit.
* The ride to most hotels in the city is very short, usually taking less than 10 minutes.
* It is advisable to agree on the fare with the driver before starting your journey, or ensure the meter is used. The cost should be very reasonable.

### Car Rental
* Picking up a pre-booked rental car at the airport is a very common and convenient choice. It provides immediate freedom to travel to the city or begin exploring the surrounding region.

### Pre-booked Transfers
* Many local hotels, guesthouses (riads), and tour operators can arrange for a driver to meet you at the airport upon arrival. This is a reliable and hassle-free option.

### Renting a Car

Renting a car is highly recommended in Ouarzazate as it provides the most flexibility to explore the city, nearby film studios, and famous kasbahs like Aït Benhaddou at your own pace.

#### Booking Process
Rental agencies are located at **Ouarzazate Airport (OZZ)** and within the city center. It is advisable to book your car online in advance, especially if you require a 4x4 for desert excursions, as availability can be limited.

* **Requirements:**
    * A valid driving license from your country of residence.
    * An International Driving Permit (IDP) may be required by some companies.
    * You will generally need to be at least 21 years old and have a credit card for the security deposit.

#### Expected Costs
Costs vary based on vehicle type and season. 4x4 vehicles will be more expensive.

* **Economy (e.g., Dacia Logan):** €30 - €45 per day
* **Compact SUV / 4x4 (e.g., Dacia Duster):** €50 - €75 per day
* **Luxury / Full-size 4x4:** €100+ per day

#### Insurance Guide
Most rentals include basic third-party liability insurance, but this often comes with a significant excess (deductible).

* **Common Options:**
    * **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW):** Reduces your financial liability for damage to the rental vehicle.
    * **Personal Accident Insurance (PAI):** Covers medical expenses for the driver and passengers. Check if your travel insurance already includes this.
* **Pro Tip:** Check with your credit card provider before you travel. Many premium cards offer rental car insurance as a benefit.

#### Driving in Ouarzazate
Driving in and around Ouarzazate is relatively straightforward and much less congested than in Morocco's larger cities.

* Drive on the **right side of the road**.
* Roads to major tourist sites are generally paved and in good condition.
* A car is essential for reaching sites like Fint Oasis and the Atlas Film Studios independently.

### Taxis in Ouarzazate

Taxis are a very common and practical way to get around Ouarzazate and are essential for reaching nearby attractions if you have not rented a car.

#### Petit Taxi (Small Taxi)
These are the standard taxis for trips within the city limits.
* **Color:** Yellow/Beige.
* **Scope:** Legally authorized for trips only within the city.
* **Pricing:** Petit taxis should use a meter (`compteur`), but it's common for drivers to negotiate a fixed price. It is best to agree on the fare before starting your journey. A short trip within town should cost between 10-20 MAD.
* **Passengers:** Up to three.

#### Grand Taxi (Large Taxi)
These larger taxis are used for longer distances, such as trips to Aït Benhaddou, Skoura, or the airport.
* **Color:** Typically yellow/beige or other light colors.
* **Scope:** Authorized for longer, inter-city, and suburban trips. They are the primary mode of transport for day trips if you don't have a rental car.
* **Pricing:** They do not use meters. You must negotiate a fixed fare for your destination or a half-day/full-day hire before departure.
* **Passengers:** Up to six.

### Historic Sites & Kasbahs

* **Aït Benhaddou**
    A UNESCO World Heritage site, Aït Benhaddou is a breathtaking ksar (fortified village) made of traditional earthen clay. It is one of the most famous and best-preserved examples of southern Moroccan architecture and has served as a filming location for numerous Hollywood movies and TV series, including *Gladiator* and *Game of Thrones*.

* **Taourirt Kasbah**
    A vast and impressive 19th-century kasbah located within the city of Ouarzazate itself. Once a stronghold of the powerful Glaoui clan, who ruled the region, its maze of rooms, passageways, and courtyards offers a fascinating glimpse into the life of a feudal caïd (governor).

* **Kasbah of Tifoultoute**
    Another historic kasbah from the Glaoui era, located just a few kilometers outside of Ouarzazate. While smaller than Taourirt, it offers commanding views of the surrounding valley and landscape.

* **The Old Synagogue**
    Located in the old Jewish quarter (mellah) near the Taourirt Kasbah, this synagogue is a testament to the rich Jewish history of the region and is maintained by the local community.

### The Cinema Museum

At the heart of Morocco's film industry is the renowned **Cinema Museum (Musée du Cinéma)**. Housed in a former film studio, this museum has traditionally offered a captivating glimpse into the world of filmmaking with its collection of sets, props, costumes, and cinematic equipment from various international productions.

---

### Museums of Cultural Heritage

#### Musée Mémoire de Ouarzazate

For those interested in the local heritage, the **Musée Mémoire de Ouarzazate**, situated in the nearby oasis town of Skoura, is a must-visit. This museum is dedicated to preserving and showcasing the rich cultural traditions of the Amazigh (Berber) people of the region. Housed within a beautifully restored kasbah, the museum's exhibits include historical artifacts, traditional clothing, tools, and manuscripts, offering a deep dive into the local way of life.

#### Taourirt Kasbah

The **Taourirt Kasbah**, while primarily a magnificent example of a fortified village, also functions as a living museum. Once a residence of the powerful Glaoui clan, visitors can now wander through its labyrinthine corridors, admire the intricate tilework and painted ceilings, and imagine life within its earthen walls. Informational panels and the sheer architectural grandeur of the kasbah provide a tangible connection to the region's history. The kasbah is generally open to visitors daily, with an entrance fee.

#### Museum of the Liberation Army

A lesser-known institution is the **Museum of the Liberation Army**. This museum is dedicated to the history of the Moroccan resistance and the fight for independence. While information about its specific exhibits and opening hours is not as readily available as for the other museums, it offers a poignant look into a significant chapter of Moroccan history.

---

### An Immersive Cinematic Experience

For a more immersive cinematic experience, many visitors also flock to the **Atlas Film Studios**, located on the outskirts of Ouarzazate. While not a traditional museum, it offers guided tours of its extensive backlots, where numerous famous films and television series have been shot. Visitors can explore replica ancient Egyptian temples, Tibetan monasteries, and other elaborate sets, making it a highlight for any film lover visiting the area. An entrance fee is required for the guided tour.

## Nightlife & Entertainment in Ouarzazate

The nightlife and entertainment scene in Ouarzazate is a reflection of its desert surroundings—it is generally quiet, relaxed, and atmospheric rather than boisterous. Evenings are typically centered around hotel bars, traditional restaurant experiences, and the unique opportunities offered by the clear desert sky.

---

### Hotel Bars & Lounges
The primary venues for evening drinks in Ouarzazate are the bars and lounges located within the city's larger hotels. These establishments cater to international visitors, offering a comfortable and safe environment to enjoy a cocktail, a glass of wine, or a beer. The atmosphere is typically subdued and perfect for a quiet conversation after a day of exploring.

### Traditional Restaurant Experiences
Many evenings in Ouarzazate revolve around a long, traditional Moroccan dinner. Numerous restaurants, particularly those within historic kasbahs or riads, offer set menus featuring classic dishes like tagine and couscous. These meals are often accompanied by live traditional Amazigh (Berber) music, creating an authentic and memorable cultural experience.

### Stargazing
One of the most spectacular forms of entertainment in the Ouarzazate region is the night sky itself. Away from the city lights, the clear, unpolluted desert skies offer breathtaking opportunities for stargazing. Many guesthouses and tour operators outside the city can arrange evenings dedicated to observing the stars, providing a peaceful and awe-inspiring way to spend the night.

In Ouarzazate, like any Moroccan city, mosques are central places of worship and important landmarks. Here is some information about the city's mosques:

### Main Mosques in Ouarzazate

* **Mohammed VI Mosque:** Often considered the largest and most modern mosque in the city, it stands out with its contemporary Islamic architecture. It is a symbol of the city's religious life.
* **Other Mosques:** Ouarzazate is home to many other mosques, such as the **Al Mouqaouma Mosque**, **Al Massira Mosque**, and **Al Wahda Mosque**. These are primarily community mosques used for the five daily prayers.

---

### Visiting Mosques as a Non-Muslim

A key point to remember in Morocco is that, with very few exceptions, mosques are places of worship reserved exclusively for Muslims. Non-Muslims are not permitted to enter the prayer halls. The main exception is the Hassan II Mosque in Casablanca, which is open to tourists for guided tours.

While you won't be able to go inside, you can still appreciate the beautiful architecture and design of Ouarzazate's mosques from the outside.

It is essential to be respectful of these places of worship. Please avoid entering the mosques and do not disturb the faithful, especially during prayer times.

Old Synagogue
Located within the historic Tassoumaat district of Ouarzazate, the Old Synagogue now serves as a museum for Jewish artifacts. It offers visitors a chance to learn about the history and culture of the Jewish community that once thrived in the region. The synagogue is a testament to the long-standing relationship between Jewish and Berber people in Morocco.

Sainte-Thérèse Church
This small Catholic church, built in 1929, was originally established to serve the French Army. Today, it still holds Mass and is a notable site for those interested in the city's colonial history and its Christian community.

## A Guide to Souks and Shopping in Ouarzazate

Shopping in Ouarzazate offers a chance to discover the rich artistic traditions of the Amazigh people of the Atlas Mountains and the desert. The city is a key center for regional crafts, known for its vibrant carpets, intricate pottery, and handcrafted silver jewelry.

---

### Traditional Souks & Markets

#### Central Market & Medina
Unlike the sprawling, walled souks of other Moroccan cities, the main market area in Ouarzazate is more of a central, open-air souk located in the city center. It's a bustling marketplace where locals shop for daily necessities, fresh produce, spices, and household goods. Within this area and the small medina, you can find numerous shops selling traditional crafts.

#### Souk opposite the Taourirt Kasbah
Directly opposite the main entrance to the historic Taourirt Kasbah, you will find a collection of bazaars and shops specifically catering to visitors. These stores offer a wide variety of souvenirs and regional crafts, making it a convenient stop after exploring the kasbah.

**Key Goods to Look For:**
* **Berber Carpets:** The region is known for its beautiful, handwoven carpets and rugs, often featuring bold geometric patterns and vibrant colors characteristic of local tribes.
* **Pottery:** Look for the distinctive green-glazed pottery from Tamegroute, a village in the nearby Draa Valley.
* **Silver Jewelry:** The area has a rich tradition of Amazigh silver jewelry, often intricate and decorated with semi-precious stones.
* **Saffron:** The nearby town of Taliouine is the heart of Morocco's saffron production, and high-quality saffron can be purchased in the souks.

---

### Artisan Cooperatives

#### Ensemble Artisanal (Artisans' Center)
Located in the city center, the Ensemble Artisanal is a government-sponsored complex that brings together artisans from the region. It's a great place to see craftspeople at work and to purchase high-quality, authentic goods at fixed prices, which eliminates the need for bargaining. You can find a wide range of products, including carpets, leather goods, stonework, and textiles. Visiting the cooperative supports the local artisans directly.

### Regional Specialties & Signature Dishes

#### Tagine
The quintessential Moroccan stew is a cornerstone of every menu in Ouarzazate. Given the region's climate, tagines are often rich and savory. Look for classic preparations like **Lamb or Beef Tagine with Prunes and Almonds**, or **Chicken Tagine with Preserved Lemons and Olives**. Many local restaurants pride themselves on their unique family recipes passed down through generations.

#### Couscous
The national dish is particularly significant in southern Morocco. It consists of steamed semolina grains served with a hearty stew of seasonal vegetables and meat. While traditionally a Friday dish, many restaurants in Ouarzazate serve it daily due to its popularity with both locals and visitors.

#### Madfouna (amazigh "Pizza")
A specialty of the desert regions, Madfouna is a large, stuffed flatbread. The dough is filled with a mixture of finely chopped meat (often beef or lamb), onions, herbs, and spices, and sometimes includes nuts or hard-boiled eggs. It's then cooked directly in hot sand or in a traditional oven, earning it the nickname "amazigh  Pizza."

#### Brochettes (Kebabs)
Grilled skewers of meat are a popular and delicious choice. Marinated in a blend of Moroccan spices, the skewers of lamb, beef, or chicken are cooked over charcoal, giving them a smoky flavor. They are often served simply with bread and salads.

####The  Tchicha
 barley semolina based soup, the Berber Tchicha is cooked with tomatoes, onions and spiced with olive oil and wild mint. It is eaten in the High Atlas mountains, especially in winter when it is very cold, and is offered to women who have just given birth for its nutritional and energetic value.

####Chorba with pigeons and nuts
 The history of this luxury dish goes back to the time of Pacha Thami El Glaoui, lord of the Atlas. Legend has it that the table of this dynasty had to be furnished with a dish that was out of the ordinary every day. The Chorba with pigeons and nuts is a very refined and extremely rich dish.

####BADDAZ 
 Rhythmed by the sun, which ripens the ingredients, Baddaz, the Berber couscous, is a variant of the Moroccan couscous with a thousand turns, which is distinguished both by its ingredients, and by its singular preparation. Its base, composed of corn semolina (Asengar) is carefully rolled by hand, then steamed in a couscoussier, three times, before being flavored with ‘smen’ (rancid butter).

#### Harira
The traditional Moroccan soup, made from a rich base of tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas, and fresh herbs. It's a staple in local eateries and is a warming and nourishing start to any meal.

#### Dates
The nearby Draa Valley is famous for its vast palm groves that produce some of the best dates in Morocco. You will find them used in both savory tagines and sweet dishes, or simply served on their own.

The site of the city of Ouarzazate keeps traces of human occupation from prehistory. Indeed, carved stone tools dating from the Paleolithic, of the Hachereaux of the Moroccan Acheulean were discovered there.
The history of Ouarzazate is that of a crossroads—a place where desert trade routes converged and where powerful tribes exerted their influence over the surrounding valleys. Its modern story is one of transformation, from a remote garrison town to the celebrated "Hollywood of Morocco."


### Early History & The Taourirt Kasbah

For centuries, Ouarzazate was not a city but a strategic point along the trans-Saharan trade routes, connecting the desert with the cities north of the Atlas Mountains. The area was dotted with fortified villages (ksour) and grand kasbahs built by local Amazigh chieftains to control the flow of goods and protect the valuable oases.

The most significant and enduring of these structures is the **Taourirt Kasbah**. Its oldest parts date back to the 17th century, but it was significantly expanded in the 19th century. This sprawling mudbrick fortress, with its labyrinth of rooms and imposing towers, is the historical heart of the city and a prime example of southern Moroccan architecture.

### The French Protectorate

During the French Protectorate (1912-1956), the French army established a garrison and administrative center in Ouarzazate in 1928. They were attracted to its strategic location, which served as a base to supervise the surrounding Draa, Dades and Ziz valleys. The French built a new, modern city alongside the traditional kasbahs, laying the foundation for the future development of the city. The French faced fierce resistance from local tribes.

### Overview of Ouarzazate's History

The provided documents trace the history of Ouarzazate from its origins as a strategic point on the trans-Saharan trade routes rather than a formal city. The area was characterized by fortified villages and kasbahs, with the Taourirt Kasbah, whose oldest parts date to the 17th century, being the most significant structure and the historical heart of the area.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the region came under the control of the powerful Glaoui clan, who expanded the Taourirt Kasbah into a grand residence and used it as a base to control trade. The city's modern layout began to form in 1928 during the French Protectorate, when the French military established a garrison and built a new administrative town next to the old kasbahs.

Ouarzazate's contemporary identity was shaped by the film industry starting in the 1960s with movies like *Lawrence of Arabia*. This led to the creation of major film studios, including Atlas Studios in 1983, and earned the city the nickname "Ouallywood". Today, Ouarzazate is a thriving tourist hub known as the "Gateway to the Desert," blending its historical role with its modern identity as a cinematic capital and a center for solar power innovation.

### The Glaoui Era

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the region came under the domination of the powerful **Glaoui clan**. Led by the brothers Madani and Thami El Glaoui, the family established a vast feudal territory across southern Morocco. The Taourirt Kasbah became one of their most important strongholds, serving as a residence and a base from which they controlled the trade of salt, saffron, and other goods. The Glaoui's immense wealth and political power, often exercised in alliance with the French, allowed them to expand the kasbah into the grand palace-like structure seen today.

### "Ouallywood": The Cinema Capital

Ouarzazate's modern identity was forged by the film industry. Its dramatic desert landscapes, stark mountain scenery, and magnificent kasbahs proved to be the perfect backdrop for epic films. Beginning in the 1960s with movies like *Lawrence of Arabia* (1962), the region became a sought-after location for international filmmakers.

This led to the establishment of major film studios, including **Atlas Studios** in 1983 and later **CLA Studios**. The city earned the nickname "Ouallywood" as it provided sets and services for a long list of blockbuster movies and TV series, including *Gladiator*, *The Mummy*, and *Game of Thrones*. This industry has become a cornerstone of the local economy.

### Ouarzazate Today

Today, Ouarzazate is a thriving city that serves as the "Gateway to the Desert." It is the primary tourist hub for travelers exploring the kasbahs of the south, the Draa Valley, and the dunes of the Sahara. The city successfully blends its ancient role as a crossroads with its modern identity as a cinematic capital, attracting tourists, adventurers, and filmmakers from around the world. In recent years, it has also become home to one of the world's largest solar power plants, adding a new layer to its identity as a center of innovation.

### Types of Accommodation

#### Riads & Guesthouses (Maisons d'Hôtes)
For an authentic and intimate experience, staying in a riad or a guesthouse is an excellent choice. These are often family-run establishments that offer personalized service and traditional Moroccan hospitality. Many are beautifully decorated with local Amazigh (Berber) crafts and feature courtyards and terraces with views of the surrounding landscape.

#### Kasbah Hotels
A truly unique accommodation option in the Ouarzazate region is the opportunity to stay in a restored kasbah. These historic mudbrick fortresses have been converted into characterful hotels, allowing guests to sleep within ancient walls while enjoying modern comforts. This type of accommodation offers a deep sense of history and place.

#### Hotels
Ouarzazate has a good selection of modern hotels that cater to various budgets. These range from larger hotels with swimming pools and extensive amenities, popular with tour groups, to smaller, more modest establishments. They are a reliable option for travelers seeking conventional comforts.

#### Apartments
For those looking for more independence or traveling in a group, renting a private apartment can be a practical option. This allows for self-catering and provides a more local living experience.
