# Rabat–Salé Airport (RBA)

## Overview

Rabat–Salé Airport (`IATA: RBA`, `ICAO: GMME`) is the international airport serving Morocco's capital city, Rabat, and its sister city, Salé. Located in Salé, approximately 8 km (5 miles) northeast of Rabat's city center, the airport serves a mix of diplomatic, business, and leisure travelers. The facility also shares its runway with a military air base of the Royal Moroccan Air Force. While not as large as the airports in Casablanca or Marrakech, it is a modern and growing facility with a strong focus on routes to Western Europe.

## Quick Facts

* **Official Name:** Rabat–Salé Airport
* **IATA Code:** `RBA`
* **ICAO Code:** `GMME`
* **Location:** Salé, Rabat-Salé-Kénitra, Morocco
* **Operator:** ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority)
* **Website:** [www.onda.ma](http://www.onda.ma/en/Our-Airports/Rabat-Sale-Airport)

## Terminals

The airport currently operates **one modern passenger terminal** that handles all domestic and international flights. The terminal was significantly modernized and expanded to increase its capacity and improve passenger experience. While plans for a much larger new terminal are underway to support future growth, the current facility is well-equipped, compact, and easy to navigate.

## Ground Transportation

Several options are available to travel from the airport in Salé to the city center of Rabat.

### Airport Bus
* **AE (Airport Express):** The dedicated shuttle from **AEROPORT-GARE AGDAL**, connecting the airport with the Agdal high-speed train station.
* The bus schedule is generally aligned with flight arrivals and departures. This is often the most convenient public transport option.

### Taxis
* **Grand Taxis** are available 24/7 from the official taxi rank located outside the arrivals hall.
* These taxis can take you directly to your destination in Rabat or Salé. It is important to agree on the fare with the driver before starting your journey, as the trip involves crossing from one city (Salé) to another (Rabat).

### Car Rental
* Renting a car is a practical option for those planning to explore the coastal region or travel between cities independently. Desks for rental agencies are easy to find upon exiting the baggage claim area.

### Renting a Car

Renting a car provides the most flexibility for exploring the city and venturing out to nearby coastal towns or historical sites.

#### Booking Process
Rental agencies are conveniently located at **Rabat–Salé Airport (RBA)** and within the city center. It's recommended to book online in advance, especially during peak travel periods, to secure better rates and ensure availability.

* **Requirements:**
    * A valid driving license from your country of residence.
    * An International Driving Permit (IDP) may be required by some agencies.
    * You must typically be at least 21 years old and possess a valid credit card.

#### Expected Costs
Costs can fluctuate based on the vehicle type, rental duration, and the season.

* **Economy (e.g., Hyundai i10):** €25 - €40 per day
* **Compact SUV (e.g., Dacia Duster):** €40 - €60 per day
* **Luxury:** €85+ per day

#### Insurance Guide
While basic third-party liability insurance is usually included, it often comes with a high excess (deductible).

* **Common Options:**
    * **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW):** Reduces your financial liability for damage to the rental car.
    * **Personal Accident Insurance (PAI):** Covers medical expenses. Check if your personal travel insurance already provides this coverage.
* **Pro Tip:** Inquire with your credit card provider, as many offer rental car insurance as a cardholder benefit, potentially saving you additional costs.

#### Driving in Rabat
Driving in Rabat is generally considered less chaotic than in larger Moroccan cities. The main avenues are wide and well-maintained.

* Drive on the **right side of the road**.
* Pay close attention at roundabouts and yield to traffic already circulating.
* Be mindful of the tramway lines and their priority.
* Parking in the city center can be challenging; look for designated paid parking lots.

### Intercity Buses

This station connects Rabat to other cities.

#### Gare Routière de Kamra (Rabat Main Bus Station)
* **Location:** Avenue Hassan II, Rabat, Morocco.
* **Description:** This is the main bus terminal for intercity services in Rabat. It serves as a central point for buses traveling to and from various cities across Morocco, including Casablanca, Marrakech, and Tangier.
* **Note:** The station hosts numerous bus companies. Major carriers like CTM and Supratours also operate from this station. It is a good practice to check schedules and book tickets in advance, especially for popular routes and during peak seasons.



Rabat, the capital of Morocco, has two main train stations, both serving as critical hubs for the national railway network.

### Gare de Rabat Agdal

* **Location:** In the modern Agdal neighborhood, south of the city center.
* **Description:** This is the city's main and most prominent train station. Opened in 2018, it is a state-of-the-art facility designed to handle high-speed train traffic. It features a sleek, modern architectural style with multiple levels, numerous shops, cafes, and a spacious waiting area. The station is a testament to Morocco's investment in its railway infrastructure.
* **Key Destinations:** Gare de Rabat Agdal serves all long-distance and high-speed train routes. It is a major stop on the **Al Boraq high-speed line**, connecting it directly to:
    * Casablanca
    * Tangier
    * Kenitra
    It also serves all major conventional train routes to:
    * Marrakech
    * Fez
    * Meknes
    * Oujda
* **Note:** If you are traveling to or from a major city, especially on the high-speed line, this is the station you will use. It's well-connected by local taxis and the Rabat Tramway to the city center and other neighborhoods.

### Gare de Rabat Ville

* **Location:** Right in the heart of the city, at the end of Avenue Mohammed V, a short walk from the medina and many of Rabat's main attractions.
* **Description:** This is the older and more central of the two stations. It has a classic, traditional Moroccan architectural design. While it has been modernized, it retains a more historic feel. It's smaller than Rabat Agdal but incredibly convenient for travelers wanting to explore the city's old town and central sights on foot.
* **Key Destinations:** Gare de Rabat Ville primarily serves regional and conventional train lines. It is a key stop on the "Train Navette Rapide" (TNR) commuter rail line. From here, you can easily catch trains to:
    * Casablanca (Casa-Voyageurs and Casa-Port)
    * Kenitra
    * Salé
* **Note:** For international or long-distance travelers, Gare de Rabat Agdal is the primary station. However, if your destination is a major tourist site in central Rabat, like the Hassan Tower, the Mausoleum of Mohammed V, or the Medina, it's often more convenient to take a regional train or a taxi to Gare de Rabat Ville.

### Overview of Transportation in Rabat

The provided documents describe the transportation network serving Rabat and its sister city, Salé, which is primarily served by the Rabat–Salé Airport (RBA), located 8 km from Rabat's city center. Ground transport options from the airport include a dedicated Airport Express (AE) bus, Grand Taxis, and car rental services.

For local travel, the modern Rabat-Salé Tramway is an efficient option, with two lines connecting key areas in both cities for a fare of 6 MAD per journey. The ALSA public bus network provides an extensive and affordable alternative, with a standard ticket costing around 5 MAD. The taxi system is divided into two distinct types: blue 'Petit Taxis' are used for metered trips within the limits of a single city (either Rabat or Salé), while larger 'Grand Taxis' are used for longer, negotiated-fare journeys, including travel between the two cities.

For intercity travel to other destinations in Morocco, the Gare Routière de Kamra serves as the main bus terminal. Renting a car is also a practical option for exploring the region, with agencies located at the airport and in the city. Driving in Rabat is on the right side of the road and is generally considered less chaotic than in other major Moroccan cities.

### Taxis in Rabat

Taxis are a popular and convenient way to get around Rabat. Always confirm the fare or use of the meter before your journey begins.

#### Petit Taxi (Small Taxi)
These are the standard taxis for trips within the city limits of either Rabat or Salé.
* **Color:** Blue.
* **Scope:** Legally authorized for trips only within a single city's limits (they cannot cross from Rabat to Salé).
* **Pricing:** All taxis are equipped with a meter, which is mandatory for calculating the fare. The meter starts at a base fare, with most trips costing between 15 and 40 MAD.
* **Passengers:** Up to three.

#### Grand Taxi (Large Taxi)
These taxis are used for longer distances, including trips between Rabat and Salé, to the airport, and to nearby towns.
* **Color:** Typically white or light-colored older Mercedes models.
* **Scope:** Authorized for longer, inter-city, and suburban trips.
* **Pricing:** They do not use meters. The fare is fixed but should be negotiated and agreed upon before departure.
* **Passengers:** Up to six, often shared with other passengers going in the same direction.

### Rabat-Salé Tramway

The tramway is a modern, efficient, and clean public transport option that connects key areas of Rabat and Salé.

#### General Information
The network consists of two lines that intersect, making it easy to navigate between the two cities and reach major hubs, residential areas, and administrative districts.

* **Fares:** A single journey ticket costs 6 MAD. Rechargeable cards are available for frequent users, offering a more economical way to travel.
* **Hours of Operation:** Trams generally run from approximately 6:00 AM to 11:00 PM, with reduced frequency on weekends and late at night.

#### Key Routes
* **Line 1:** This line connects the Hay Karima neighborhood in Salé to the Madinat Al Irfane district in Rabat. Key stops include the **Gare de Salé** (train station), **Mohammed V - Gare de Rabat** (Rabat-Ville train station), **Bibliothèque Nationale** (National Library), and the university district of **Madinat Al Irfane**.
* **Line 2:** This line connects the Hôpital Moulay Abdellah in Salé to the Yacoub Al Mansour neighborhood in Rabat. It passes through important hubs like **Hassan II (Gare routière)** (main bus station), **Bab Chellah**, and **Médina Rabat**.
* **Connection Point:** Both lines connect at the **Bab Lamrissa**, **Pont Hassan II**, **Place du 16 Novembre**, **Tour Hassan**, and **Place Al Joulane** stations, allowing for easy transfers.

### Historic & Architectural Landmarks

* **Kasbah des Oudaias**: Get lost in the charming blue-and-white painted streets of this 12th-century fortress overlooking the Atlantic Ocean and Bou Regreg river. The main gate is the monumental **Porte Des Oudayas**.
* **Hassan Tower & Mausoleum of Mohammad V**: Visit the city's most iconic landmarks. The **Hassan Tower** is the impressive, unfinished minaret of a 12th-century mosque. Adjacent to it is the magnificent **Mausoleum of Mohammad V**, the final resting place of three former Moroccan kings.
* **Chellah**: Explore the fascinating ruins of this medieval fortified necropolis, which contains both Roman ruins and an Islamic sanctuary. The site is accessed through the **Bab Chellah** gate.
* **Rabat Old Town (Medina)**: Wander through the bustling streets of the historic medina to experience local life, shop for crafts, and discover hidden alleyways.
* **Royal Palace of Rabat**: Admire the impressive architecture and grand squares of the official residence of the King of Morocco, though access to the interior is restricted.
* **Religious Sites**: Discover the city's diverse religious heritage by visiting the Art Deco **Cathedrale Saint-Pierre**, the central **Assounna Mosque**, and the historic **Russian Orthodox Church**.
* **Bab Mellah**: Explore the historic Jewish Quarter of the city.


### Historic Sites & Viewpoints

* **Kasbah of the Udayas (Kasbah des Oudaias)**
    This is a picturesque 12th-century fortress and residential neighborhood at the mouth of the Bou Regreg river. Its narrow, blue-and-white painted streets, monumental gate (Bab Oudaia), and stunning views of the Atlantic Ocean make it one of Rabat's most charming and famous landmarks.

* **Hassan Tower**
    The iconic, unfinished minaret of a mosque commissioned in the late 12th century by the Almohad Caliph Yaqub al-Mansur. Intended to be the largest minaret in the world, its construction was stopped upon the sultan's death. The impressive tower and the forest of stone columns on the site are a testament to the scale of this grand project.

* **Mausoleum of Mohammed V**
    Located across from the Hassan Tower, this is a masterpiece of modern Alaouite dynasty architecture. It contains the tombs of the Moroccan King Mohammed V and his two sons, King Hassan II and Prince Abdallah. The building is renowned for its striking white silhouette, green-tiled roof, and richly decorated interior.

* **Chellah**
    A unique and atmospheric archaeological site that contains the ruins of both the ancient Roman city of Sala Colonia and a medieval Islamic necropolis. The fortified complex is now a tranquil garden, where visitors can wander among ancient tombs, minarets, and Roman remnants, often accompanied by the resident storks that nest on the ruins.

## Nightlife & Entertainment in Rabat

As Morocco's capital, Rabat offers a more relaxed and sophisticated nightlife scene compared to other Moroccan cities. The entertainment here is less about sprawling nightclubs and more focused on chic lounge bars, trendy restaurants, and cultural events, reflecting the city's cosmopolitan and diplomatic character.

---

### Lounges & Bars

The city's modern nightlife is primarily concentrated in its upscale neighborhoods.

* **Agdal**: This is the heart of Rabat's trendier nightlife scene. The area is packed with modern, Western-style venues, bustling bars, and restaurants that transition into lively social spots as the evening progresses. It's a popular destination for the city's younger crowd.
* **Hay Riad**: This modern business district is home to several upscale lounges and clubs. 
* **City Center (Ville Nouvelle)**: The downtown area features more established venues, including hotel bars and classic spots, known for its piano music.

A unique venue is **Le Dhow**, a restaurant and lounge on a traditional wooden boat moored on the Bouregreg River, offering drinks with stunning views of the historic cityscape.

### Nightclubs

While Rabat is not primarily a nightclub destination, it does have a few key spots for those looking to dance.

### Cultural Entertainment

Rabat's status as a cultural capital means there is a strong emphasis on evening cultural activities.

* **Live Music**: Several venues offer live music, from jazz to various genres at local pubs and cultural hubs like the **Goethe Institute**.
* **Festivals**: The city hosts major international music festivals, most notably **Mawazine - Rhythms of the World**, which brings global and local stars to stages across the city. The **Jazz au Chellah** festival blends European jazz with Moroccan music in the city's historic ruins.
* **Theaters**: The **Théâtre National Mohammed V** is a major venue for concerts and performances throughout the year.

### Popular Neighborhoods for Tourists

#### The Medina
The historic heart of the city, Rabat's Medina is a UNESCO World Heritage site. Staying here means you are surrounded by traditional markets (souks), historic walls, and within walking distance of key attractions like the Kasbah of the Udayas and the Bou Regreg river. It's the best area for a culturally immersive experience and is where most of the city's beautiful riads are located.

#### Hassan / Ville Nouvelle (Downtown)
This is the central, modern part of the city, built during the French Protectorate. It is characterized by wide, palm-lined avenues, Art Deco architecture, and key landmarks like the Hassan Tower and the Mausoleum of Mohammed V. This area has a high concentration of hotels, restaurants, cafes, and shops, making it a convenient and central base for exploring.

#### Agdal
A vibrant, upscale residential and commercial neighborhood popular with Rabat's middle and upper class. Agdal is known for its trendy shopping streets, modern cafes, international restaurants, and lively atmosphere. It's a great choice for travelers who want to experience the modern, dynamic side of Rabat while still being well-connected to the main tourist sites.

#### Hay Riad
Hay Riad is Rabat's newest and most modern business district. It is characterized by its wide boulevards, contemporary architecture, luxury hotels, and high-end restaurants. While it's located further from the historic center, it's an excellent choice for business travelers or those seeking a sleek, quiet, and upscale environment.

#### L'Océan
Situated along the Atlantic coast, this historic residential neighborhood offers a more local, laid-back feel. It's close to the ocean and provides a less touristy experience, with easy access to beaches and the main city sights via a short taxi ride.

Rabat, Morocco, offers a variety of religious and historical sites that reflect the country's diverse cultural and religious heritage. Here are some of the notable places to visit:

### Islamic Sites

* **Hassan Tower and the Mausoleum of Mohammed V:** This is a major historical and religious complex. The Hassan Tower is the minaret of an incomplete mosque, started in the 12th century. It was intended to be the largest minaret in the world. The Mausoleum of Mohammed V, located opposite the tower, is a modern masterpiece of traditional Moroccan architecture. It is the final resting place of King Mohammed V and his sons, King Hassan II and Prince Abdallah.
* **As-Sunna Mosque (Assounna Mosque):** Built in the 18th century, this historic mosque is a significant example of Moroccan Islamic architecture.

### Jewish Sites

* **The Mellah (Jewish Quarter):** This historic walled quarter was established in the 19th century and was once home to Rabat's Jewish community. While now mostly inhabited by Muslims, it still holds significant Jewish heritage.
* **Rabbi Shalom Zaoui Synagogue:** Located in the Mellah, this synagogue is a beautiful example of Moorish architecture. It was once the home of Rabbi Shalom Zaoui and is still an active place of worship.
* **Rabat Jewish Cemetery:** The old Jewish cemetery is a notable site with tombstones inscribed in Hebrew, French, and Spanish. It contains the tombs of revered saints, including Eliezer de Avila and Chalom Zaoui.

### Christian Sites

* **St. Peter's Cathedral (Cathédrale Saint-Pierre de Rabat):** This Roman Catholic church is a prominent landmark in downtown Rabat. Built in the early 20th century, it features a distinctive Art Deco style and is the ecclesiastical seat of the Archdiocese of Rabat.

Shopping in Rabat offers a diverse experience, from the labyrinthine alleys of the historic Medina to the chic, modern boutiques in the Ville Nouvelle and Agdal. ### Traditional Souks & Markets

#### The Medina
The walled Old City, or Medina, of Rabat is a UNESCO World Heritage site and the primary destination for traditional shopping. The main thoroughfare is the bustling **Rue des Consuls**, a covered street historically designated for foreign merchants to conduct their business.

**Key Features:**
* **Rue des Consuls**: This is the most famous shopping street in the Medina, known for its high-quality artisan goods. It's an excellent place to find traditional Moroccan carpets, leather goods (especially babouches), intricate brass and copperware, and fine textiles.
* **Souk Es Sebat**: Another major artery in the Medina, this covered market specializes in traditional clothing like kaftans and djellabas, as well as jewelry and other crafts.
* The Medina's souks are generally more relaxed and less aggressive than those in Marrakech or Fes, allowing for a more pleasant Browse experience.

#### Marché Central (Central Market)
Located in the Ville Nouvelle, Rabat's Central Market is a vibrant, covered market hall where locals do their daily shopping. It's a fantastic place to experience local life and see a vast array of fresh produce, meat, spices, olives, and flowers.

The modern downtown area, built during the French Protectorate, offers a European-style shopping experience.

### Signature Moroccan Dishes in Rabat

#### Tagine
The classic slow-cooked Moroccan stew is a staple in Rabat. You will find a wide array of options in the city's restaurants, from the savory **Chicken Tagine with Preserved Lemons and Olives** to the sweet and savory **Lamb Tagine with Prunes and Almonds**. Given its coastal location, fresh **Fish Tagine** is also a popular and delicious local choice.

#### Couscous
The national dish of Morocco, couscous is a hearty meal of steamed semolina grains served with a rich stew of vegetables and meat (typically lamb, beef, or chicken). It is traditionally served on Fridays, and many restaurants in Rabat offer it as a special on this day, prepared with care and expertise.

#### Pastilla (B'stilla)
This intricate and flavorful pie is a highlight of Moroccan cuisine. It consists of flaky 'warqa' pastry filled with spiced pigeon or chicken, layered with almonds, and dusted with cinnamon and powdered sugar. It's a festive dish that showcases the complexity of Moroccan flavors.

#### Harira
The traditional and beloved Moroccan soup, made from a hearty base of tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas, and fresh herbs. It is a staple in local eateries and is often the first dish served to break the fast during Ramadan.

#### Brochettes (Kebabs)
Grilled meat skewers are a popular choice for a quick and delicious meal. Found in both street-side stalls and formal restaurants, they are typically made from lamb, beef, or chicken, marinated in Moroccan spices, and grilled over charcoal for a smoky flavor.

#### Fresh Seafood
Being on the Atlantic coast, Rabat offers excellent fresh seafood. Local restaurants, particularly those near the coast and the mouth of the Bou Regreg river, serve fresh catches of the day, from grilled fish to fried calamari, often seasoned with a zesty 'chermoula' marinade.

### Early History and Almohad Glory

While the Romans had a settlement called Sala Colonia on a nearby site (now the Chellah), the history of Rabat proper began in the 12th century. The Almohad Caliph **Abd al-Mu'min** established a 'ribat' (a fortified monastery or fortress) on the site of the current Kasbah of the Udayas. This ribat served as a base for his armies as they launched campaigns into Spain.

The city's golden age came under Abd al-Mu'min's grandson, **Abu Yusuf Yaqub al-Mansur**, in the late 12th century. He envisioned a grand capital city, which he named **Ribat al-Fath** ("The Fortress of Victory"). He embarked on an ambitious building program, constructing the magnificent gate of the Kasbah (Bab Oudaia) and expanding the city walls. His most ambitious project was a colossal mosque intended to be the largest in the world, of which only the impressive, unfinished **Hassan Tower** and its forest of stone columns remain. After al-Mansur's death, the project was abandoned, and Rabat entered a long period of decline.

### The French Protectorate and Modern Capital

Rabat's modern history began in 1912 with the establishment of the **French Protectorate**. The French administrator, **Hubert Lyautey**, made the momentous decision to designate Rabat as the administrative capital of the protectorate. He felt its coastal location was more practical than the interior cities of Fes or Marrakech.

Lyautey commissioned the French architect and urban planner **Henri Prost** to design a modern administrative quarter, the **Ville Nouvelle**, adjacent to the old medina. Prost created a new city with wide, tree-lined avenues, elegant ministerial buildings, and public gardens, carefully preserving Rabat's historic landmarks. This period defined the layout and architectural character of modern Rabat.

### The Republic of Salé

For centuries, Rabat was overshadowed by its neighbor, Salé. The city's fortunes were revived in the early 17th century with the arrival of thousands of **Moriscos** (Muslims expelled from Spain). These new inhabitants, possessing advanced skills in seafaring and warfare, settled in the old Kasbah and established an independent city-state known as the **Republic of Salé**.

For several decades, this republic became a notorious and powerful base for the **Barbary Pirates** (also known as the Salé Rovers). These pirates raided ships as far away as the British Isles and Iceland, capturing goods and enslaving crews, making the republic a significant maritime power.

---

### Alaouite Rule and Imperial Status

The pirate republic was eventually brought under the control of the central Moroccan government by the **Alaouite dynasty** in the late 17th century. The Alaouites unified Rabat and Salé and recognized Rabat's importance. Although sultans moved the capital between cities like Fes and Meknes, Rabat was often used as an imperial residence.

### Special Events

In 2012, UNESCO officially inscribed "Rabat, Modern Capital and Historic City: A Shared Heritage" on the World Heritage List, confirming the exceptional universal value of the capital of the Kingdom of Morocco. This idyllic location, overlooking the Atlantic Ocean on one side, on the left bank of the Bouregreg River estuary, boasts a prehistoric human settlement with a rich and prosperous past, traces of which the city has preserved to this day.

Designed at the beginning of the 20th century, the modern city of Rabat represents one of the largest and most ambitious urban projects undertaken in Africa in the past century, and perhaps the most complete to date. Fortunately, care has been taken to preserve the existing heritage.

Thus, the 1,200 hectares covering the UNESCO-listed site and its buffer zone preserve traces of all periods of the history of the Atlantic coast and more broadly of Morocco, from ancient times (Phoenicians, Mauritanians, Romans) to the 20th century (French protectorate, independent Morocco), passing through the various medieval eras (Berguatas, Almoravids, Almohads, Merinids, Saadians) and the modern and contemporary era (Alawites).

### Rabat Today

After Morocco gained independence in 1956, **King Mohammed V** made the decision to keep Rabat as the nation's capital. It became the permanent home of the royal family, the government, and foreign embassies.

In 2012, **UNESCO** recognized Rabat's unique urban landscape by awarding it World Heritage status. The listing highlights the city's exceptional blend of its historic Almohad and Andalusian past with the modernist colonial architecture of the 20th century. Today, Rabat is celebrated for its clean, green spaces, its relaxed atmosphere, and its rich collection of historical monuments, serving as the political and administrative heart of the nation.
