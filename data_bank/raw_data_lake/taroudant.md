Taroudant, while known for its historic walls and bustling souks, also offers a number of tranquil green spaces and gardens that provide a peaceful escape from the city's vibrant atmosphere. These areas are ideal for relaxation, a quiet stroll, or enjoying the natural beauty of the Souss Valley.

### Gardens and Green Spaces within the City

* **Jardin du Riad Taroudant:** Many of the city's beautiful riads and guesthouses are centered around lush inner gardens. These private gardens, often filled with citrus trees, bougainvillea, and fragrant jasmine, are a key feature of the accommodation and offer a quiet sanctuary for guests.
* **Public Gardens and Squares:** While not extensive parks, the public squares like **Place Assarag** are adorned with palm trees and flowers, providing a pleasant setting to sit and people-watch. The city's main avenues also feature rows of trees and some small, well-maintained green spaces.

### Gardens and Oases Around Taroudant

The true natural beauty and leisure opportunities lie just outside the city walls in the surrounding landscape.

* **The Palmeraie (Palm Grove):** The area immediately surrounding the city is known for its extensive palm groves. Taking a walk, riding a donkey, or even a horse-drawn carriage through the **palmeraie** is a popular leisure activity. These groves offer a tranquil, shaded environment perfect for a leisurely afternoon.
* **Tiout Oasis:** Located about 25 kilometers from Taroudant, the Tiout Oasis is a spectacular and popular day-trip destination. This lush palm grove is situated at the foot of a stunning kasbah that offers panoramic views of the entire oasis. Visitors can explore the oasis on foot or by a guided donkey ride through the palm trees and ancient irrigation channels. Many tours include a traditional lunch at a local restaurant overlooking the grove.
* **Afgou Oasis:** Further into the Anti-Atlas Mountains, the Afgou Oasis offers another beautiful natural escape. This is a more rugged, less-visited location, ideal for those who enjoy hiking and discovering remote natural landscapes.

While Taroudant may not have large, formal public parks like those found in major metropolitan cities, its charm lies in its intimate riad gardens and the vast, natural oases that surround it, providing unique opportunities for relaxation and exploration.

--- Content from: [city]/leisure/museums.txt ---

Taroudant is a city known for its vibrant street life, traditional souks, and historic architecture rather than for its formal museums. While it does not have large, public museums in the traditional sense, there are a few notable cultural spaces and collections that offer insight into the region's art and history.

### The Claudio Bravo Palace (Fondation Claudio Bravo Camus)
This is arguably the most significant cultural site near Taroudant. Located on a vast estate just outside the city, the palace was the home and studio of the late Chilean hyperrealist painter, Claudio Bravo.

* **What to See:** The palace itself is an architectural marvel, blending traditional Moroccan design with modern elements. It houses an extensive collection of Bravo's works, showcasing his mastery of still life, portraits, and nudes. Visitors can also explore the beautifully maintained gardens, which feature exotic plants, fountains, and sculptures.
* **Experience:** Visiting the palace is a unique opportunity to appreciate world-class art in a stunning and private setting. It functions as a de facto museum and is a must-visit for art lovers.

### Art Galleries
Taroudant's traditional arts scene is more focused on the work of local artisans in the souks. However, some riads and guesthouses have small, private art galleries or exhibition spaces showcasing the work of local and international artists. These spaces often feature paintings, sculptures, and photographs inspired by the city and its surroundings.

### Historical Sites
For a deeper understanding of the city's past, the historical sites themselves serve as a kind of living museum.
* **The City Walls (Ramparts):** The monumental 16th-century walls are the city's most impressive historical artifact. Walking or riding along them gives you a direct connection to Taroudant's history as a fortified Saadian capital.
* **The Great Mosque:** While not a museum, its Saadian architecture and historical significance as a center of learning make it an important cultural landmark.
* **The Tanneries:** Located just outside the city walls, the tanneries are a living exhibition of a traditional Moroccan craft that has been practiced for centuries. Visitors can observe the process of leather-making from start to finish.

In summary, while Taroudant lacks the formal, large-scale museums of cities like Marrakech or Fez, its artistic and historical heritage is best experienced through its preserved architecture, the living art of its souks, and the impressive collection at the Claudio Bravo Palace.

Taroudant, often called "Little Marrakech," offers a more relaxed and authentic Moroccan experience. The city's leisure activities are centered around its historical medina, vibrant souks, and beautiful natural surroundings.

### Exploring the City

* **City Walls (Ramparts):** The city is encircled by ancient, ochre-colored ramparts that are approximately 7.5 kilometers long. A popular activity is to walk, bike, or take a horse-drawn carriage ride along these walls. At certain points, you can climb the ramparts for panoramic views of the city and the surrounding Anti-Atlas Mountains, especially at sunset.
* **Souks (Markets):** Taroudant is known for its two main souks: the Arab Souk and the Berber Souk. These markets are a great place to experience daily Moroccan life and shop for traditional handicrafts. You'll find a wide variety of goods, including leatherwork, carpets, silver Berber jewelry, spices, and local argan oil.
* **Assarag Square and Place 20 Ghoucht:** These are the two main plazas in the medina, offering a great starting point for exploring. They are lively hubs where you can sit at a cafe, sip mint tea, and watch the world go by.
* **Traditional Hammam:** For a relaxing and cultural experience, visit a local hammam (public bathhouse). This is a traditional way to unwind and cleanse.

### Day Trips and Outdoor Activities

* **Tiout Oasis:** Located about 30 kilometers from Taroudant, the Tiout Oasis is a stunning palm grove at the foot of an old kasbah. You can explore the lush greenery on foot or by a donkey ride, and enjoy a traditional lunch with a view of the oasis from the kasbah.
* **Hiking and Trekking:** Taroudant is a gateway to the High Atlas and Anti-Atlas Mountains. You can arrange guided hiking or trekking tours for one or more days to explore the rugged landscapes, traditional Berber villages, and beautiful valleys.
* **Horseback Riding and Quad Biking:** For a more adventurous outing, you can go horseback riding or quad biking to explore the landscapes around the city, including argan tree forests and dry riverbeds (wadis).
* **Claudio Bravo Palace:** Visit the stunning palace and gardens of the late Chilean painter, Claudio Bravo, which showcase a beautiful collection of art and exotic plants.

### Culture and History

* **Tanneries:** Just outside the city walls, you can visit the local tanneries to see the traditional process of leather making. The leather goods in the souks are often sourced from here.
* **Architecture:** The city's historical sites include the Grand Mosque and the Arrahma Mosque, which offer a glimpse into the city's past and Islamic architecture.
* **Local Cuisine:** Taroudant offers a chance to enjoy authentic Moroccan food. Be sure to try local pastries and traditional dishes like tagine.

Taroudant's leisure activities are primarily concentrated within its historic medina and the areas immediately surrounding the ancient city walls. Unlike larger cities with distinct, modern neighborhoods, the main points of interest for visitors are centered on the traditional parts of the city.

### Inside the Medina (Old City)

The medina is the heart and soul of Taroudant and the most popular area for visitors. It is a dense network of winding alleyways and historical sites.

* **Place Assarag and Place 20 Ghoucht:** These two main squares are the central hubs of the medina. They are ideal for people-watching, sipping a mint tea at a cafe, and experiencing the daily life of the city. Place Assarag is often compared to a smaller, more relaxed version of Marrakech's Jemaa el-Fnaa.
* **The Souks:** The medina houses two main souks (markets) that are a must-visit. The **Arab Souk**, located along Rue Bin-Anzarane, is known for its handicrafts, spices, silver jewelry, and leather goods. The **amazigh  Souk** (or Souk Jnane al-Jaami) is a more traditional market selling fresh produce, clothes, and everyday household items.
* **The Kasbah:** The historic citadel, located in the northern part of the medina near Bab El Kasbah, was once the administrative center of the city. Today, it is a residential area with narrow streets and a very authentic atmosphere.
* **The Tanneries:** Located just outside the city walls near the Bab Targhount gate, the tanneries offer a glimpse into the traditional leather-making process.

### Outside the City Walls

The area surrounding the ramparts provides different leisure opportunities, from walks to cultural experiences.

* **The Ramparts:** The most prominent feature of Taroudant is its nearly 7.5-kilometer-long city walls. A popular activity is to walk, bike, or take a horse-drawn carriage ride along the outside of the walls, especially at sunset.
* **Gardens and Oases:** The area around the city is known for its lush palm groves and gardens, which are perfect for a relaxing stroll or a quiet picnic. The **Tiout Oasis**, located about 25 km away, is a famous day trip destination offering a tranquil setting and a traditional meal in a stunning palm grove.
* **The Palmeraie:** Just outside the walls, especially near the Kasbah, you can find palm groves where you can walk or ride a donkey to experience the rural landscape.

For a true Taroudant experience, focus your time on exploring the medina's inner life and enjoying the unique perspective offered by the city's monumental walls.

The mosques of Taroudant

* **The Great Mosque of Taroudant, named Al Aâdham:
Renovated by Muhammed ach-Chaykh of the Saadian dynasty around the middle of the 16th century, this mosque is one of the most important and largest of the Saadian era.Located to the east of the city, this mosque currently covers an area of 3,215 square meters and can accommodate up to 4,000 worshippers. It is distinguished by its 27-meter tall minaret. This square minaret is one of the most famous in Morocco. The mosque is also characterized by a richly decorated mihrab adorned with kufic letters. A place of worship, but also of teaching, the Great Mosque includes a library, once endowed with an important collection of manuscripts and precious documents that have been patiently stored over the centuries.

* **The Kasbah Mosque:
 is considered one of the prominent historical landmarks in the city, and it was greatly affected by the earthquake that struck the region in September 2023. Since then, restoration and repair works have remained pending, raising concerns among residents who view the mosque as an important part of their religious and cultural heritage.


* **The Mosque of Farq al-Ahbab:
 is located in the city center in the neighborhood of (Farq al-Ahbab), with an area of about 1460 square meters. It was founded by Sultan Abdullah al-Ghalib bin Muhammad al-Sheikh al-Saadi in the second half of the 10th century AH / 16th century AD and was named the new mosque, with shops nearby and gardens outside the city walls.One of its unique features is that its minaret was built above its southern entrance. Meanwhile, its western door, opposite the mosque’s ablution area, was small and was known as the Door of (Al-Hafa), referring to the removal of shoes by those entering the mosque courtyard after washing and cleaning their feet from the nearby fountain, and this name was also used for the large arched door that has now vanished.


* **Mosquée Sidi Ou Sidi
It is associated with the name of the righteous saint Abu Muhammad Salih bin Wandalus, who died after the year 1195 AD and is known among the people of the city as (Sidi and Sidi). Perhaps his shrine was there, indicating that the establishment of the mosque dates back to before the 10Th century AH/16Th century AD.He added that the area of the mosque is estimated to be about 460 square meters, and the height of its minaret is approximately 13 and a half meters. It was renovated by Sultan Moulay Slimane Alawi at the beginning of the 19th century. The construction of the "Sidi and Sidi" mosque adhered to the preservation of the unique Moroccan architectural style in all its manifestations, with authentic Moroccan decoration characterized by wood carving, local tiles, and plaster.This religious landmark features a wooden dome located in the center of the mosque, crafted with precision and high craftsmanship, long windows distinguished by their heritage with a unique shape, and an ornately decorated mihrab inscribed .


### Koranic Schools and Islamic Education

Traditional education has always been an essential component of the cultural and religious life of Taroudant. Since the 16th century, the city has been a center for Islamic learning, as evidenced by the **Madrasa of the Great Mosque**, a place for the study of religious jurisprudence and sciences.

In the modern era, this tradition continued with the founding of the **Islamic Institute in 1959** by King Mohammed V. This institute was created to preserve and promote Islamic education, playing a major role in the training of religious scholars and teachers in the region.



* **Jewish Religious and Historical Sites

* **Jewish Cemetery: Taroudant had a significant Jewish community that played an important role in the city's caravan trade. While the community has largely emigrated, there are still remnants of their history. The Jewish cemetery in Taroudant, and another one in the nearby Tiout Oasis, is a place to reflect on this history.

* ** Tombs of Jewish Saints: The region around Taroudant holds the tombs of several Jewish saints, which are considered pilgrimage sites. These include the tomb of Rabbi Shalom Benzaquin in Tiout and the tomb of Rabbi Daoud Ben Baroukh in Souk Tlat de Mnahba, on the road to the Tizi n'Test pass.

* ** Former Synagogue: Taroudant's old synagogue has reportedly been transformed into an antique shop. This gives a glimpse into the city's past and the coexistence of different communities.

Specialties & Signature Dishes
 
 Tagine
This slow-cooked stew is a national dish, and in taroudant , the Fish Tagine (Tagine de Poisson) is a local specialty. This version often features firm local fish like monkfish (lotte) or conger eel, cooked with tomatoes, potatoes, peppers, and a flavorful 'chermoula' marinade. You will also find classic variations with lamb or chicken, often simmered with preserved lemon and olives.

Amlou
A delicious and energizing spread that is a true specialty of the Souss region. It is a thick, brown paste made from roasted almonds, pure culinary Argan oil, and honey. It's typically served for breakfast or as a snack and used as a dip for bread.
 
 
Culinary Argan Oil (Huile d'Argan Alimentaire)
The Argan tree is endemic to the region around Agadir, making its culinary oil a fundamental local ingredient. It has a distinct, nutty flavor from roasting the kernels and is used as a finishing oil drizzled over salads and tagines, or simply as a dip for bread.
 
Tafarnout Bread
A large, round, and somewhat flat traditional Amazigh (Berber) bread known for its chewy texture and rustic flavor. It is traditionally baked on a bed of hot stones or in a communal clay oven and is the perfect vessel for dipping in tagine, Argan oil, or Amlou.
 
Tagoula
A hearty and simple Berber porridge made from barley semolina. It is considered a staple of Amazigh cuisine in the region and is typically served in a large communal dish with a well created in the center, which is then filled with Argan oil, Amlou, or smen (fermented butter) for dipping.
 
Couscous
The national dish of Morocco, often served with vegetables and meat. Friday is traditionally "couscous day," so you'll find it fresh and abundant on this day.
 
Harira
A hearty, traditional Moroccan soup made with lentils, chickpeas, and tomatoes. It is often served with a soft-boiled egg, dates, and a sweet chebakia cookie.


Tanjia roudania
The word Tanjia refers both to a traditional dish and the clay pot in which it is cooked. This bulbous pot is hermetically sealed and buried under the hot ashes of the hammam or a wood-fired oven for slow cooking, without direct flame. The result: melt-in-your-mouth lamb or veal, slowly simmered with spices, preserved lemon, garlic, olive oil, and smen (butter).

The History of taroudant

If there is disagreement among historians about the date of the founding of the city of Taroudant, they all agree that the foundation of the city is attributed to the emirs of the Hachtouka and Jazoula tribes, and recognize that Taroudant was a center of political and commercial activity for the Soussi tribes.Taroudant, the capital of the Shiite Bajjalis kingdom in the 11th century, the first capital of the Saadians in the 16th century, a refuge for rebel princes, coveted by the southern tribes, destroyed in 1306 by the Merinids, is one of the oldest cities in Morocco and holds a prominent place in the history of the country. It played a very important role in the history of Souss and Morocco in general, both during the pre-Islamic period and through the various stages of Muslim Maghreb history.
At the start of the sixteenth century, Taroudant experienced a revival in its economic significance and strategic role due to the rise of the Saadian dynasty. Mohammed Sheikh was instrumental in this resurgence, as he constructed several monuments that shaped the city's architectural identity; he restored and strengthened the city walls, built the kasbah, the grand mosque, and an educational institution. Additionally, the outskirts of Taroudant saw the establishment of notable sugar factories, contributing to its status as a key economic and cultural center.
As the influence of the Saadian state waned, the city became a site of local conflicts until the Alawite state was established, marked by Moulay Rashid's entry in 1669-1670. After the death of Moulay Ismail, the city faced instability until Sidi Mohammed Ben Abdellah received allegiance, during which the entire Sous region was beyond central control. The efforts of Moulay Hassan I were significant in reestablishing the state's authority over this area, including Taroudant.
The French occupation left many landmarks that commemorate its presence in the city in the early 20th century, including the Paul Chatinier Hospital (currently Al-Mokhtar Souissi) and the Public Learning School (Ibn Souleiman Al-Roudani High School) within the old city. They were established together at the beginning of the second decade of the 20th century in 1922. The French also created a European city outside the walls to the northeast between the Kasbah wall and the observation point area. It includes several administrative facilities from the French colonization such as the post office, which played an important role in telecommunication and securing the administrative postal system, the police headquarters, the management of forest and woodland properties, and the French school (Al-Mansour Al-Thahabi) in 1942.
The city spent difficult years under the influence of French civil affairs officers, who represented the French protectorate, which led its educated youth to engage in resistance.
In 1959, King Mohammed V laid the foundation stone for the construction of the Islamic Institute. In 1981, King Hassan II, as part of a policy to bring the administration closer to citizens, reinstated Taroudant as a distinguished base for Souss, by issuing an order to make this city the capital of the Taroudant region, according to royal decree No. 2-81-854 dated 2 Safar 1402, corresponding to December 18, 1981.

The history of Taroudant during the French occupation is a reflection of the resistance that marked southern Morocco. Unlike other regions of the country that were "pacified" relatively early, the Souss region and the area around Taroudant experienced fierce and prolonged resistance.

### General Context of the French Occupation in Morocco

In 1912, France established its protectorate over Morocco with the signing of the Treaty of Fez. However, this treaty did not end the resistance. In many regions, especially in rural and mountainous areas, local tribes continued to fiercely oppose the French presence.

### Resistance in Taroudant and the Souss

* **A late defeat of the resistance:** Taroudant and the Souss region were strongholds of resistance against the colonizer. The French only established effective control over the city in 1917, several years after the beginning of the protectorate.
* **The role of Ahmed al-Hiba:** The resistance in southern Morocco was led by figures like Ahmed al-Hiba, a tribal and religious leader. In 1912, he managed to subdue a large part of the Souss region and take control of Agadir and Taroudant. He even marched on Marrakech but was defeated by French forces at the Battle of Sidi Bou Othman in 1912. He withdrew to the south and continued the resistance until his death in 1919.
* **Control and administration:** Once control was established, the protectorate authorities set up a local administration. For example, a pacha (governor) was appointed, often chosen from influential local families known for their loyalty to France.
* **The impact of the occupation:** Under the protectorate, the role of Taroudant changed somewhat. While it had been a caravan city and a historical capital of the Saadian dynasty, its economic importance declined in favor of coastal cities like Agadir. However, it retained its character as a traditional city of the Souss.

The occupation of Taroudant and the Souss region illustrates the complexity and violence of the French "pacification," which lasted for several decades and faced determined resistance from the local population.

--- Content from: [city]/history/overview.txt ---
Taroudant is a city with a long and storied history, often referred to as "Little Marrakech" due to its impressive red-earth walls and traditional souks. Its historical significance is rooted in its strategic location in the fertile Souss Valley, making it a key player in trans-Saharan trade and a powerful political center.

### Origins and Early History

While the exact founding date is debated, it is believed that Taroudant existed as a fortified Roman outpost known as **Vala**. It later served as the capital for several local dynasties and rebel princes, a testament to its enduring importance in the region.

### The Saadian Golden Age

Taroudant's most glorious period was in the 16th century when it was the capital of the Saadian dynasty, under Sultan Mohammed al-Sheikh. The Saadians used Taroudant as a base to defend against the Portuguese presence in nearby Agadir and to establish their power over southern Morocco. During this time, the city prospered, becoming a major hub for the trade of goods like sugar, gold, and salt.

### The City Walls

The most visible legacy of the Saadian period is the city's magnificent ramparts. Built in the 16th century, the walls are approximately 7.5 kilometers long and feature a series of gates, towers, and bastions. These formidable fortifications made Taroudant a virtually impregnable citadel for centuries. The walls are not only a defensive structure but also a powerful symbol of the city's history and a key part of its identity.

### Decline and Rebirth

Taroudant's prosperity declined in the 18th century after the port of Agadir was closed to foreign trade by the Alaouite dynasty. The city receded from the national stage, turning inward and preserving its traditional character. It was a center of fierce resistance against the French protectorate in the early 20th century, holding out against colonial forces until 1917.

Today, Taroudant has maintained its authenticity. It is a vibrant market town that serves as the economic hub for the surrounding region, offering visitors a window into a part of Morocco that has largely been untouched by mass tourism.

Taroudant, with its long and rich history, has been a key location for several significant events and continues to be a hub for cultural celebrations. While its most defining historical moments are tied to its political and military past, the city today is a vibrant center for art and tradition.

### Historical Events

* **The Saadian Golden Age:** The most significant period in Taroudant's history was the 16th century when it served as the capital of the Saadian dynasty under Sultan Mohammed al-Sheikh. This was a time of great prosperity, as the city became a major center for the trade of gold, sugar, and salt, and its iconic ramparts were built during this era to fortify it.
* **A Center of Resistance:** Taroudant played a crucial role in the early 20th century resistance against the French protectorate. The city served as a key base for the resistance leader Ahmed al-Hiba, who used it as a capital to oppose the French forces. The city's inhabitants and its surrounding tribes put up a fierce fight, and it was one of the last areas in Morocco to fall under French control, not being "pacified" until 1917.

### Modern Special Events and Festivals

Today, Taroudant's special events are focused on celebrating its rich cultural heritage, particularly its Amazigh (Berber) and musical traditions.

* **Festival National de l'Art Griha et Malhoun:** This national festival is dedicated to the art of Griha and Malhoun, which are traditional forms of Moroccan poetry and music. The event, which takes place in the city, aims to preserve and promote this cultural heritage, with performances, tributes to artists, and exhibitions.
* **Izourane Traditional Arts Festival:** The province of Taroudant hosts this festival, which celebrates traditional Amazigh arts, with a particular focus on the collective dance known as *Ahwach*. The event brings together artistic troupes from different regions and includes dance performances, music concerts, and workshops. It is a key event for preserving and showcasing Amazigh culture.
* **Taroudant Horse Festival:** As an important agricultural and horse-rearing region, Taroudant also hosts a horse festival. This event highlights the deep-rooted connection between Moroccans and their steeds, often featuring *Fantasia* performances, a traditional exhibition of horsemanship.
