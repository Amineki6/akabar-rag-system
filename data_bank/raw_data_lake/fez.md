# FEZ ( Fes Sais) Airport

## Overview

Fes-Saïss Airport (FEZ)(IATA: FEZ, ICAO: GMFF)  is an international airport located approximately 15 kilometers southwest of the city of Fes, Morocco. 

## Quick Facts

* **Official Name:** Fes-Saïss Airport
* **IATA Code:** `FEZ`
* **ICAO Code:** `GMFF`
* **Location:** fez , Morocco
* **Operator:** ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority)
* **Website:** [www.onda.ma](http://www.onda.ma/en/Our-Airports/fes-saiss-Airport)

---

## Terminals
The airport has two terminals, with Terminal 1 being the main terminal for international flights and Terminal 2 for domestic flights. It offers a range of facilities for passengers including restaurants, cafes, duty-free shops, currency exchange, and ATMs.


---

## Airlines and Destinations

It is one of the busiest airports in Morocco, serving as a hub for several airlines including Royal Air Maroc, Ryanair, and Air Arabia Maroc.

---

## Passenger Services and Facilities

The terminal offers a range of essential services for travelers.

* **Dining and Shopping:** A selection of cafés and snack bars are available both before and after security. A duty-free shop in the international departures area offers perfumes, tobacco, alcohol, and Moroccan goods.
* **Financial Services:** Currency exchange counters and ATMs are available in the main hall.
* **Lounges:** A VIP lounge ("Pearl Lounge") is available for eligible passengers or those wishing to pay for access.
* **Car Rental:** Several international and local car rental companies have service desks located in the arrivals hall.
* **Wi-Fi:** Free Wi-Fi is accessible throughout the terminal building.

---

## Ground Transportation

Several options are available to travel from the airport in fes-saiss  to the city center of fez.

### Airport Bus
* **AE (Airport Express):*The airport Fez Saiss is located about 15 km from the city center of Fez. The bus (number 16) offers regular trips between the airport and the bus and train stations of Fez. The trip takes 40 mins from the airport to the central train station. Taxis are available for hire at the exit of the passenger terminal. The rate to the city centre is fixed at 150 dirhams.

* The bus schedule is generally aligned with flight arrivals and departures. This is often the most convenient public transport option.

### Taxis
* **Grand Taxis** are available 24/7 from the official taxi rank located outside the arrivals hall.
* Taxi is a good transportation option to get from FEZ airport to your destination. The journey time to the city center is 25 minutes.


### Car Rental
* Renting a car is a practical option for those who plan to explore the area or travel independently between cities. The rental agency counters are easy to find upon exiting the baggage claim area.

### Historic & Architectural Landmarks

* **Discover Fez Medina.
Fez is a city with a rich history, serving as a center of culture and learning in Morocco for centuries. Its historic core, a UNESCO World Heritage site, is a remarkable example of a well-preserved medieval city. The architecture of Fez is a blend of various influences, including Arab, Berber, and Andalusian styles, and is known for its intricate details and traditional craftsmanship.



### Historic Sites & Viewpoints

Here are some of the most prominent historic and architectural landmarks in Fez:

Religious and Educational Institutions
***Al-Qarawiyyin Mosque and University: 
Founded in 859 AD, this is one of the oldest continually operating universities in the world. It is a major landmark of Islamic learning and architecture, known for its intricate tile work and large hypostyle prayer hall. While non-Muslims cannot enter the prayer hall, the exterior and courtyard are stunning.

***Bou Inania Madrasa: 
Built in the mid-14th century, this madrasa is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful in Fez. It is a masterpiece of Marinid architecture, featuring stunning zellige tilework, carved stucco, and intricate cedar wood carvings. It is one of the few religious sites in Morocco that is open to non-Muslim visitors.

***Al-Attarine Madrasa: 
Dating back to the 14th century, this madrasa is another prime example of Marinid architectural art. It is smaller than the Bou Inania Madrasa but equally impressive, with a peaceful courtyard and exquisite decorative details.

***Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss II:
 This mausoleum is the burial site of Idris II, the founder of Fez. It is a revered and sacred site for Moroccans and is known for its intricate tilework and spiritual atmosphere.

***City Walls and Gates:
Bab Bou Jeloud (The Blue Gate): This iconic city gate is one of the main entrances to the Fes el-Bali medina. Known for its beautiful blue and green tiles, it serves as a symbol of the city and a gateway to its bustling markets and historic streets.

***City Walls and Forts:
 Fez is surrounded by high, fortified walls with numerous historic gates (babs). The city's defensive architecture also includes forts like Borj Nord and Borj Sud, which offer panoramic views of the medina.

***Here are some of the most important gates in Fez:

Fes el-Bali (the old city)
***Bab Bou Jeloud (the Blue Gate): This is undoubtedly the most famous and photographed gate in Fez. Built in 1913, it is recognizable by its blue ceramic tiles on the outside and green ones on the inside, which lead directly into the lively medina.

***Bab Mahrouk: Located to the west of the old city, this 13th-century Almohad gate is one of the oldest still standing. It is more austere and robust than the Blue Gate, and its name means "the Burned Gate," evoking its history.

***Bab Ftouh: This gate is located in the southeast of the medina and is one of the most important historic gates. It opens onto the cemetery of the same name.

***Bab Guissa: Located in the northeast of Fes el-Bali, this Almohad gate allows passage between the medina and the outer quarters.

Fes el-Jdid (the new city)
***Bab Dekkakin: This monumental gate, which is part of the royal city's fortification system, is situated between the Old and New Mechouar. Its name means "Gate of the Benches" and refers to the benches where visitors would wait to be received at the palace.

***Bab Semmarine: Rebuilt in 1924, this high arched gate is a key entry point to Fes el-Jdid.

These gates, in addition to their utilitarian role, represent the architectural magnificence of Fez and mark the passage between the different eras and quarters of the city.


***Palaces 
Royal Palace of Fez (Dar al-Makhzen): While the palace itself is not open to the public, its ornate exterior and impressive golden gates are a major architectural sight. The palace is an active residence of the King of Morocco.

Other Notable Landmarks
***Chouara Tannery: 
As one of the oldest and largest tanneries in Fez, the Chouara Tannery is a unique and famous landmark. Visitors can observe the traditional leather dyeing process from the terraces of surrounding shops, with colorful vats creating a memorable spectacle.

***The Mellah (Jewish Quarter):
 The historic Jewish quarter of Fez is notable for its distinct architecture, including houses with wooden balconies and a number of historic synagogues, such as the Ibn Danan Synagogue.

### Museums & Galleries


***Dar Batha Museum: Housed in a former 19th-century palace, this museum showcases a collection of traditional Moroccan arts and crafts, including ceramics, textiles, and woodwork. The palace itself is a work of art, with a beautiful Andalusian-style garden.

*** Nejjarine Museum of Wooden Arts and Crafts: Located in a beautifully restored caravanserai (a traditional inn for merchants), this museum features intricate woodwork and a stunning central courtyard.

***Borj Nord Museum of Arms: Situated in a 16th-century fortress, this military museum offers a unique perspective on Moroccan history. It houses an impressive collection of weapons from different periods, including swords, daggers, and cannons, with some pieces dating back to the Roman and Ottoman eras. The fort also provides panoramic views of the entire Fez medina.

Other Notable Cultural Sites
***Bou Inania Madrasa: While primarily a historic Islamic school and a stunning example of Marinid architecture, the Bou Inania Madrasa functions like an open-air museum. Visitors can admire the intricate carved stucco, cedar woodwork, and beautiful zellige that adorn its walls and courtyard. It is one of the few religious buildings in Fez open to non-Muslim visitors.

***Al-Attarine Madrasa: Like the Bou Inania, this 14th-century madrasa is a breathtaking example of architectural art. Its smaller size and delicate details, including finely carved cedar and plaster, make it a memorable visit.

***Ibn Danan Synagogue: Located in the Mellah (the historic Jewish quarter), this is one of the oldest synagogues in Fez. Restored in the 1990s, it offers a glimpse into the city's rich Jewish heritage.

Art Galleries
Fez also has a growing contemporary art scene. While less numerous than the historic museums, a few galleries are worth seeking out:

***Palais Sheherazade Art Gallery: This art gallery is located within a 19th-century palace, showcasing a variety of work by national and international artists. It combines the historic ambiance of the palace with modern and contemporary art.

***Alfahd Gallery: This is a contemporary art space that promotes visual arts in various disciplines, including painting, sculpture, and photography. It also hosts cultural events and workshops.

## Nightlife & Entertainment in fez

Fez offers a more traditional and subdued nightlife compared to cities like Marrakech or Casablanca. The focus is less on raucous clubs and more on cultural experiences, relaxing lounges, and dining with live entertainment. The nightlife scene is a blend of traditional Moroccan charm and a few modern venues, often found within the larger hotels and stylish riads.

Here's a breakdown of nightlife and entertainment in Fez:

### Cultural & Traditional Experiences

* **Dinner Shows:** Many upscale restaurants and riads offer evening dinner shows that provide an immersive cultural experience. These typically include a multi-course Moroccan meal, often with live performances of traditional music, belly dancing, or folk dancing. These shows are a great way to experience Moroccan hospitality and artistic heritage in a beautiful setting.
* **Traditional Music:** Fez is a center for traditional Moroccan and Sufi music. Some venues host performances of Andalusian orchestras, Sufi chanting (like the Aissawa brotherhood), or rhythmic Gnawa music. These are often held in intimate, candle-lit settings, creating a very special atmosphere.
* **Evening Strolls in the Medina:** The Fez medina, with its labyrinthine alleys, takes on a different, more tranquil character at night. The crowds thin out, and the soft glow of lanterns illuminates the streets. It's a perfect time for a peaceful walk, a cup of mint tea at a small café, or some late-night souvenir shopping at the stalls that stay open.

### Bars and Lounges

While bars and clubs are not as common as in other cities, there are several options, especially in the Ville Nouvelle (the new city) and within luxury hotels.

* **Rooftop Bars:** Many of the most popular evening spots in Fez are rooftop bars, particularly those associated with stylish riads or boutique hotels. They offer a relaxed ambiance, often with ambient music, and spectacular panoramic views of the medina, which is particularly enchanting at sunset as the city lights begin to twinkle.
    * **Mezzanine:** This is a popular spot with a rooftop terrace that offers a relaxed atmosphere and a selection of tapas, drinks, and occasional live music.
    * **Hotel Bars:** The larger, more modern hotels in the Ville Nouvelle are the primary locations for traditional bars and lounges. 

### Nighttime Activities

* **Cooking Classes:** Many guesthouses and tour operators offer evening cooking classes where you can learn to prepare traditional Moroccan dishes. These classes often end with a shared dinner, providing a social and delicious way to spend an evening.
* **Food Stalls and Souks:** The areas around Bab Bou Jeloud and Talaa Kebira in the medina have food stalls that remain active late into the evening. This is a great way to sample local street food and soak up the lively, bustling atmosphere.
* **Relaxing at a Café:** Moroccan culture revolves around socializing in cafés. Evenings are a perfect time to sit in a local café, sip on a sweet mint tea or a coffee, and people-watch as the city winds down.

### Cultural Entertainment

Fez is a city where culture is not just seen, but experienced. The entertainment scene is less about late-night revelry and more about immersive, authentic encounters with Moroccan traditions. From ancient music to hands-on workshops, Fez offers a rich tapestry of cultural activities.

### Music and Performance

* **Traditional Moroccan Music:** Fez is a major hub for traditional music genres, including Andalusian, Malhoun, and Sufi music. Many restaurants and guesthouses host live performances, often in the evening. The music is an integral part of the dining experience, providing a beautiful backdrop of rhythmic and melodic sound.
* **Sufi and Gnawa Music:** For a truly unique and spiritual experience, seek out a performance of Sufi or Gnawa music. Sufi music is often described as mystical and meditative, while Gnawa is known for its trance-inducing rhythms and hypnotic chants. These performances are not just for entertainment but are a significant part of the culture's spiritual traditions. While some performances can be found in small venues, they are most prominent during the city's festivals.
* **Dinner Shows:** Many of the most luxurious riads and restaurants in Fez offer dinner shows that combine a multi-course Moroccan feast with a show featuring dancers, musicians, and storytellers. These are a great option for a curated evening of cultural immersion and fine dining.

### Festivals

Fez is world-renowned for its annual festivals, which are a major draw for tourists and a source of national pride.

* **Fez Festival of World Sacred Music:** Held annually in late May or early June, this festival brings together musicians and artists from around the globe to celebrate the spiritual power of music. Performances take place in historic venues across the city, including the grand Bab Al Makina courtyard. UNESCO has recognized the festival for its contribution to intercultural dialogue.
* **Fez Festival of Sufi Culture:
This festival, usually held in October, is a week-long celebration of the mystical dimensions of Islam. It features a program of concerts, intellectual discussions, and spiritual ceremonies, providing a deep dive into Sufism's message of peace and unity.

***The Amazigh New Year Celebration Festival .This festival is organized by the Aman Association for Sustainable Development in partnership with the Fez-Meknes Regional Council. It celebrates the African dimension of Amazigh culture and reached its third edition in 2018, corresponding to 2968 in the Amazigh calendar.

*** The National Festival of Listening and Praise ArtOrganized by the municipality of Fez in partnership with the Fez-Meknes Regional Council, this event celebrates the arts of listening and praise across the Kingdom. This art is characteristic of cities in the region such as Fez, Meknes, and Taza, and the festival reached its twenty-second edition in 2019.


### Hands-on Workshops and Classes

To truly connect with the local culture, consider a hands-on workshop.

* **Moroccan Cooking Classes:** Fez is a paradise for food lovers, and a cooking class is an excellent way to learn about the city's culinary traditions. Many classes begin with a guided tour of the local souk (market) to select fresh ingredients, followed by a hands-on session where you'll learn to prepare classic dishes like tagine, couscous, or pastilla.
* **Artisan Workshops:** Fez is famous for its traditional crafts. Look for workshops where you can learn about the art of mosaic tile (*zellige*), leatherwork, or pottery. These experiences offer a deeper appreciation for the skills that have been passed down through generations of artisans.
* **Mint Tea Ceremonies:** Learning to prepare Moroccan mint tea is a cultural ritual in itself. Many guesthouses offer a demonstration or a short workshop on the traditional method of making and serving this staple beverage, a cornerstone of Moroccan hospitality.

### Overview of fez's Attractions

Fez is one of Morocco's oldest and most historically significant cities, widely regarded as the country's cultural and spiritual heart. Its main draw is the labyrinthine medina, a UNESCO World Heritage site, which offers an immersive journey back in time.
 
**Fassi Zellige** is one of the oldest and most famous types of traditional mosaic in Morocco, considered a jewel of Moroccan and Islamic architectural art. It is distinguished by its unique technique, vibrant colors, and complex geometric designs, and is an integral part of the cultural heritage of the city of Fez, which is celebrated as the capital of this art form.

### History and Origins of Fassi Zellige

The art of zellige dates back to the Middle Ages and flourished in Morocco and Andalusia. The craft reached its peak in the city of Fez during the Marinid dynasty in the 13th and 14th centuries, where it was used to adorn palaces, mosques, and madrasas (schools). Fassi artisans excelled in creating intricate and harmonious geometric patterns, which made Fassi zellige globally renowned for its high quality and extreme precision.

### Characteristics of Fassi Zellige

What distinguishes Fassi zellige from other types of mosaics are several factors:

1.  **Entirely Manual Technique**: Every piece of zellige is prepared completely by hand. The process begins with kneading the Fassi clay (either white or red), then shaping it into square or rectangular tiles. After drying and firing, the tiles are manually glazed with traditional colors, such as cobalt blue, green, white, and yellow.
2.  **The Cutting Tool (Minqash)**: A "minqash"—a small hammer with a sharp chisel head—is used to cut the glazed square tiles into small pieces (**fetats**) of various geometric shapes (such as squares, stars, triangles, and rhombuses). This process requires immense skill and precision.
3.  **Complex Geometric Patterns**: Fassi zellige is famous for its intricate, interwoven geometric patterns, which follow precise mathematical rules. The most well-known of these are the eight-pointed or twelve-pointed stars, which form magnificent and complex artistic compositions.
4.  **Traditional Colors**: The colors used are natural, derived from materials like cobalt (for blue), copper (for green), and manganese oxide (for black). These colors give Fassi zellige a distinctive luster.

### The Production Process

The creation of Fassi zellige is an art in itself and involves several meticulous stages:

* **Clay and Firing**: The clay is prepared and shaped into tiles.
* **Glazing**: The tiles are coated with a layer of colored glaze.
* **Cutting (Naqsh)**: The "Maalem" (master artisan) cuts the colored tiles into small geometric shapes using the minqash.
* **Assembly (Farsh)**: The small pieces are arranged on the floor or wall, embedded in a plaster-and-lime mixture, to create the final tableau. The assembly is done from the back, as the artisan follows the pattern without seeing the colored front directly.

Fassi zellige is used to decorate the floors and walls of mosques, riads, palaces, and fountains, adding a touch of timeless beauty and splendor that reflects the greatness of traditional Moroccan art.


### The Moroccan caftan is a traditional Moroccan women's costume. It is considered one of the oldest traditional clothes in the world for Moroccans. Its appearance dates back to the twelfth century during the era of the Almohad state in Morocco. It is considered a symbol of nobility, elegance, heritage and identity in Morocco, especially on happy occasions such as weddings, henna and circumcision.
The Moroccan caftan is no longer confined within the borders of Morocco. Rather, it has become the focus of the world's attention, as it has appeared in international fashion shows and been worn by prominent figures on various occasions.
Caftans and Moroccan craftsmanship
The caftan is made by skilled craftsmen, using silk, velvet, and hand embroidery with gold and silver threads, making it a piece of art that represents the Moroccan craftsmanship heritage.

### Popular Neighborhoods for Tourists

Fez is divided into three main parts, each offering a distinct experience for tourists. While most visitors spend the majority of their time in the old medina, the other neighborhoods provide a contrast in atmosphere, architecture, and attractions.

Here is an overview of the popular neighborhoods for tourists in Fez:

*** Fes el-Bali (The Old Medina)
This is the heart and soul of Fez and a UNESCO World Heritage site. It's a vast, chaotic, and beautiful medieval labyrinth where most of the city's historical landmarks, artisan workshops, and riad accommodations are located. This car-free zone is where tourists are likely to spend the most time.

Highlights:

*** Historical Attractions: The famous Chouara Tannery, Al-Qarawiyyin Mosque and University, Bou Inania and Al-Attarine Madrasas, and the Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss II are all found here.

*** Souks and Markets: The medina is a dense network of souks (markets), each specializing in a different craft, from leather and ceramics to spices and textiles. It's a fantastic place to shop for authentic Moroccan goods.

Accommodations: This is the best neighborhood for staying in a traditional Moroccan riad. These restored mansions offer a tranquil escape from the bustling streets.

Atmosphere: Fes el-Bali is a sensory overload in the best way possible—filled with the sights, sounds, and smells of traditional life that have changed little over centuries.

*** Fes el-Jdid (The New City)
Founded in the 13th century, Fes el-Jdid is not "new" in a modern sense but serves as a bridge between the ancient medina and the modern city. It is home to significant historical sites and provides a different kind of architectural experience.

Highlights:

*** The Royal Palace: While you cannot enter the palace itself, the exterior and its stunning, ornate golden gates are a major architectural attraction.

*** The Mellah (Jewish Quarter): This historic quarter is known for its distinct architecture, including houses with wooden balconies, and is home to the beautifully restored Ibn Danan Synagogue and a large Jewish Cemetery.

*** Jnan Sbil Gardens: Located near the medina, this beautiful public garden is a tranquil escape with fountains, ponds, and shaded walkways, perfect for a peaceful stroll.

 *** Ville Nouvelle (The New City)
Developed during the French protectorate in the early 20th century, the Ville Nouvelle offers a complete contrast to the medinas. It has wider streets, modern shops, cafes, and restaurants. While it lacks the historical charm of the old city, it is a practical and convenient neighborhood for tourists.

Highlights:

*** Shopping and Dining: This area features modern shops, department stores, and a wider variety of restaurants, including international cuisines. It is a good place to find a more conventional nightlife scene.

*** Hotels: Many of Fez's larger, modern hotels are located here, offering a different kind of accommodation than the riads of the medina.

Convenience: The Ville Nouvelle is where you will find banks, ATM machines, and modern amenities. It is also the main transportation hub with the train station and bus terminal.ride.

Fez is renowned as the spiritual and religious heart of Morocco. The city's medina is filled with numerous religious sites that are not only places of worship but also architectural masterpieces and centers of learning. It is important to be respectful when visiting these sites, and to note that many mosques and active shrines are not open to non-Muslim visitors.

Here are the most significant religious sites in Fez:

### Madrasas (Islamic Schools)

These historic schools are architectural jewels and are generally open to the public, offering a fascinating glimpse into Islamic art and education.

* **Bou Inania Madrasa:** Located in the heart of the medina, this is arguably the most famous and well-preserved madrasa in Fez. A masterpiece of Marinid architecture from the 14th century, it is known for its exquisite *zellige* (mosaic tilework), intricate carved cedar wood, and detailed stucco. It is one of the few religious buildings in the city that non-Muslims can enter.
* **Al-Attarine Madrasa:** Dating back to the 14th century, this madrasa is another prime example of Marinid art. Its small, peaceful courtyard and stunning decorative details—including *zellige*, carved plaster, and cedar carvings—make it a must-visit. It's often praised for its harmony and elegance.

### Mosques and Mausoleums

These sites are central to the city's religious life. While most are not accessible to non-Muslims, their exteriors and courtyards are often visible and beautiful.

* **Al-Qarawiyyin Mosque and University:** Founded in 859 AD, this is one of the oldest continuously operating universities in the world. As one of Morocco's largest mosques, it is a key landmark of Islamic scholarship. Non-Muslims are not permitted to enter the prayer hall, but you can see the expansive courtyard and the intricate architecture from several of its gates.
* **Mausoleum of Moulay Idriss II:** This mausoleum is the tomb of Idris II, the founder of Fez, who is revered as the city's patron saint. It is a highly sacred site for Moroccans. While only Muslims can enter, the exterior is magnificent, and you can feel the spiritual atmosphere from the streets that lead to its gates.
* **The Andalusian Mosque:** Located in the Andalusian quarter of the medina, this mosque was founded in the 9th century. Like other major mosques, its interior is reserved for Muslims, but its large green-tiled minaret is an important feature of the Fez skyline.

*** The Bou Jeloud Mosque (Arabic: جامع بوجلود) is a historic Almohad-era mosque in the former Kasbah of Bou Jeloud, located near Bab Bou Jeloud, in Fes, Morocco.

*** The Kasbah An-Nouar or Kasbah Filali is a walled district and former military enclosure in the old medina of Fez, Morocco. Its name means "citadel of the flowers", but it is sometimes also referred to as Kasbah Filala and Kasbah Chorfa.[1] It is one of several fortified military enclosures or kasbahs that were built around the old city of Fez across different periods.


***The Great Mosque of Fes el-Jdid (Arabic: الجامع الكبير) is the historic main Friday mosque of Fes el-Jdid, the royal city and Marinid-era citadel of Fes, Morocco. It is believed to have been founded in 1276, around the same time that the city itself was founded, making it the oldest mosque in Fes el-Jdid.

*** The Chrabliyine Mosque (Arabic: جامع الشرابليين, romanized: jama' ash-shirabliyyin; also transliterated as Shirabliyyin, Cherabliyine, etc.) is a Marinid-era mosque in Fez, Morocco. 

### Jewish Religious Sites

Fez has a historic Jewish quarter, the Mellah, which is home to notable religious sites.

* **Ibn Danan Synagogue:** Located in the Mellah, this synagogue is a testament to Fez's long-standing Jewish community. Dating back to the 17th century, it has been beautifully restored and is a powerful symbol of the city's diverse religious history. It is open to visitors and is a key site for exploring the Jewish heritage of Morocco.

## A Guide to Souks and Shopping in fez

Shopping in Fez is a truly immersive and traditional experience, with the city's medina serving as one of the largest and most authentic marketplaces in the world. The souks of Fes el-Bali are not just for tourists; they are the vibrant commercial heart of the city, where locals buy everything from fresh produce to traditional crafts.

### What to Buy in Fez

Fez is particularly famous for a few key artisan products that make for excellent souvenirs:

* **Leather Goods:** This is the city's most renowned craft. The **Chouara Tannery** is the place to witness the ancient, labor-intensive process of dyeing hides in massive vats. Surrounding the tanneries, shops offer a wide array of leather products, including high-quality bags, wallets, belts, and the iconic Moroccan poufs and *babouches* (leather slippers).
* **Pottery and Ceramics:** Fez is known for its distinctive blue and white pottery, which features intricate geometric patterns. You can find everything from small decorative items to large tagine pots and serving platters. For the most authentic experience, visit the pottery cooperative in **Ain Nokbi**, an artisan quarter outside the medina, where you can see the pottery being made from local gray clay.
* **Metalwork:** Artisans in Fez are masters of metalwork, especially copper and brass. The **Seffarine Square** is the center for coppersmiths, where you can hear the rhythmic hammering of artisans crafting trays, lanterns, teapots, and other decorative items.
* **Textiles and Rugs:** You can find a vast selection of rugs, blankets, and textiles. From intricate, colorful Berber rugs to sophisticated city-style carpets, there is a wide range of styles. If you're buying a rug, remember that bargaining is expected, and it's a good idea to know some of the basics of quality (e.g., smaller knots generally indicate higher quality).
* **Spices and Food:** The souks are a feast for the senses, with stalls piled high with vibrant spices, dried fruits, olives, and mint. **Souk Attarine** is famous for its fragrant spices, herbs, and traditional remedies.

### Popular Souks and Shopping Areas

The souks in Fez are often organized by guild or trade, so you can find specific types of goods in certain areas.

* **Souk al-Attarine:** Known as the "Spice Souk," it's a fragrant and colorful place where you can buy spices, perfumes, and beauty products like henna and argan oil.
* **Seffarine Square:** The "Coppersmiths' Souk" is located here, where you can watch artisans at work and purchase a wide variety of metal goods.
* **Souk Nejjarine:** This souk is dedicated to woodworking. It's located near the Nejjarine Museum and Fountain, and you'll find beautiful carved cedar pieces, from furniture to decorative boxes.
* **Souk Henna:** One of the oldest marketplaces in the medina, this souk sells henna, traditional cosmetics, and ceramics.

### Shopping Tips

* **Bargaining:** Haggling is a fundamental part of the shopping experience in Fez. Start by offering around 50-60% of the asking price and negotiate politely. Don't be afraid to walk away if you can't agree on a price; the vendor may call you back with a better offer.
* **Cash is King:** Most vendors in the medina only accept cash (Moroccan Dirham), so it's essential to have a supply of small bills.
* **Getting Lost is Part of the Fun:** The souks are a maze of winding alleyways, and getting lost is almost inevitable. It’s part of the adventure. You can always ask for directions or hire a guide to help you navigate.
* **Authenticity:** For high-quality, authentic goods, consider shopping at artisan cooperatives or government-certified shops.

The city of Fez is known for its ancient and luxurious cuisine, which is famous for its delicious and diverse dishes that reflect its long history and rich culture. Traditional Fassi cuisine perfectly balances sweet and savory flavors and uses spices with great precision.

Here are some of the most popular Fassi dishes, sweets, and snacks:

Savory Dishes
*** Pastilla:
Considered the most famous dish in Fez, Pastilla is a symbol of Fassi cuisine. It is a savory-sweet pie made with flaky warqa (phyllo) pastry, filled with a mixture of chicken or pigeon meat, almonds, and eggs. What makes it unique is the perfect balance between sweet and savory flavors, as it is served topped with powdered sugar and cinnamon.

*** Fassi Couscous (Couscous Fassi):
While couscous is found all over Morocco, the Fassi version has its own unique touch. It is often prepared with chicken or lamb and is garnished with a rich mixture of seasonal vegetables, chickpeas, caramelized onions, and raisins. Its flavor is known for being richer and more complex than other regional varieties.

 *** Tagine:
Although tagine is a staple throughout Morocco, Fez has its own special preparations. Fassi tagines are famous for slow-cooked lamb with prunes and almonds, or chicken with preserved lemons and olives. Both are characterized by their deep, rich flavors.

*** Harira:
Fassi Harira is a rich and nourishing soup, a fundamental dish during the month of Ramadan. It is made from tomatoes, chickpeas, lentils, and small pieces of meat. Fassi Harira is often thicker and richer than other versions and is usually served with dates and chebakia (a type of Ramadan sweet).

*** Trid or Rfissa:
This dish consists of very thin, flaky crepes that are torn into small pieces and soaked in a rich broth made with chicken or pigeon meat, lentils, and onions. This traditional dish is often served on special occasions.

Fassi Sweets
*** Chebakia: This is a famous Moroccan sweet, particularly popular during Ramadan. It is a flower-shaped pastry that is fried and then soaked in honey, often sprinkled with sesame seeds. It's known for its sweet, honey-rich flavor and crunchy texture.

***Fekkas: These are twice-baked cookies that resemble Italian biscotti. They come in various flavors, from almond and anise to raisin and pistachio. They are a popular sweet to serve with Moroccan mint tea.

***Kaab el Ghazal: Meaning "Gazelle's Horns," these crescent-shaped pastries are filled with a delicate almond paste and are known for their elegant look and soft, chewy texture.

***Sfenj
Sfenj: This is a classic Moroccan donut-like fried bread. Made from a simple dough, it is deep-fried to create a light, airy interior with a crispy, golden-brown exterior. It is often enjoyed as a breakfast treat or a snack, either plain, sprinkled with sugar, or dipped in honey. It is a very popular street food.

### The French Protectorate and Modern Capital

The cantoning of Fes within its walls continued until the establishment of the French protectorate in Morocco, which marked a new stage in the urban history of Fes. ### Fez in the time of the Almohads
The Idrisid state is considered the first independent Islamic state in the history of Morocco, which made Fes its capital and a major cultural and civilizational center.
Then the Almoravids took control of the city, as Fez witnessed urban and economic development during the reign of Yusuf ibn Tashfin in the 5th century AH (the first decade of the Gregorian calendar), who worked to unify both banks of the city within a single wall, and contributed to the revival of economic life by building hotels, baths, and mills.
The fortifications of Fez date to the eleventh century period of Almoravid rule (1056-1147). During the Almoravid reign the two separate cities of the Qayrawani and the Andalusi, which faced each other across the Oued Fez river, were united by a single fortification. Under the Almohads (r. 1130-1269), these ramparts were destroyed and then rebuilt. This fortification was completed by the Marinid dynasty (r. 1196-1549) following the founding of the new administrative center, Fez al-Jadid (New Fez) in 1276.
After a nine-month siege, the Almohads took the city in the year (538 AH = 1143 AD), and its development accelerated during the Almohad era in the 12th and 13th centuries AD, as the Almohad mosque became a university and a center for intellectual and religious currents.
The Banu Marin took control of the city after the fall of the Almohad State; this urban, economic, and cultural development of Fez encouraged the Merinids to choose it as the capital of their kingdom.
Despite the transfer of the government capital to Marrakech, the Saadi dynasty distinguished the city of Fes for its enormous achievements, such as the construction of the corridors of the Al-Qarawiyyin mosque and a number of palaces, the restoration of the city walls, and the construction of two large towers on the north and south sides of the city of Fes.
In (1077 AH = 1666 AD), during the reign of Mawla Rashid, the founder of the Alaouite state, Fez remained the capital of the kingdom before his brother Mawla Ismail chose Meknes as the new capital of the kingdom.

### fez Today

Today, Fez exists as a living testament to its long and storied history, where the past and present coexist in a unique blend of tradition and modernity. The city's current identity is shaped by its role as a UNESCO World Heritage site, its economic reliance on tourism and traditional craftsmanship, and its ongoing efforts to balance preservation with contemporary life.

Preservation and Heritage
*** UNESCO World Heritage Site: The Old Medina (Fes el-Bali) was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1981. This status has been a driving force behind major restoration and preservation projects. International and national organizations are actively involved in restoring historical buildings, including homes, riads, and religious schools, to protect the city's unique architectural heritage.

*** Artisan Revival: Fez's traditional crafts, such as leather tanning, pottery, and metalwork, continue to thrive. While these crafts have faced challenges, they are a central part of the city's economy and cultural identity. Many workshops are still run by families who have passed down skills for generations. The city is a hub for high-quality, authentic Moroccan handicrafts.

Economic and Social Life
*** Tourism: Tourism is the most significant pillar of Fez's modern economy. Visitors from around the world are drawn to the medina's authenticity, historical sites, and vibrant souks. The growth of tourism has led to the transformation of many historic houses into guesthouses (riads) and boutique hotels.

*** The Ville Nouvelle: The modern section of Fez, built during the French Protectorate, serves as the city's administrative and commercial hub. It provides a stark contrast to the medina, with wider streets, modern shops, universities, and commercial services. This division reflects the city's duality, as the modern city supports the daily life of its residents while the old medina remains the cultural heart.

*** Fez's Role in Modern Morocco
Cultural and Intellectual Center: Fez retains its historic role as a center of learning and spirituality. The Al-Qarawiyyin University is still an active institution, and the city continues to attract students and scholars of Islamic and Moroccan studies. The annual Fez Festival of World Sacred Music is an internationally renowned event that reinforces the city's cultural prominence.

### Types of Accommodation
Fez offers a diverse range of accommodation options, with each providing a different experience depending on your budget and preference. The choice between staying in the old medina (Fes el-Bali) or the new city (Ville Nouvelle) is often the most important decision for visitors.

This is the most popular and authentic type of accommodation in Fez, offering a unique and immersive stay inside the historic medina. A **riad** is a traditional Moroccan house with a central courtyard or garden. Most riads are beautifully restored mansions with intricate tilework (*zellige*), carved plaster, and an open-air central space. Staying in a riad provides a quiet and elegant escape from the bustling city streets. A **dar** is similar to a riad but is a smaller, traditional home with a courtyard but without a garden. Many have also been converted into guesthouses.

### 2. Hotels (Medina and Ville Nouvelle)

Hotels in Fez vary widely, from small, boutique hotels to large international chains. Some hotels are located inside the medina, offering a more modern alternative to a riad while staying close to the historic sites. In the Ville Nouvelle, you will find most of the large, modern hotels, including international chains. These hotels are located on wide boulevards and offer amenities like swimming pools, bars, and restaurants.

### 3. Hostels (Budget-Friendly)

Fez has a growing number of hostels that cater to backpackers and budget travelers. Most are located in the medina and are often found in small, renovated traditional houses. They offer dormitory-style rooms, communal kitchens, and common areas, providing a social environment for travelers to meet.

### 4. Vacation Rentals 

For those who prefer a self-catering option or are traveling in a group, vacation rentals are a great choice. Websites offer everything from single rooms in a local's house to entire riads or apartments for rent. This option provides more space and privacy.
