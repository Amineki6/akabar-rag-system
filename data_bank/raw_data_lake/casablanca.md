# Mohammed V International Airport (CMN)

## Overview

Mohammed V International Airport (`IATA: CMN`, `ICAO: GMMN`) is the busiest airport in Morocco and the primary international gateway to the country. Located in the province of Nouaceur, approximately 30 km south-east of Casablanca, it serves as the main hub for the national carrier, Royal Air Maroc. As a major transit point for flights between Europe, Africa, and North America, CMN is one of the most significant aviation hubs on the African continent.

## Quick Facts

* **Official Name:** Mohammed V International Airport
* **IATA Code:** `CMN`
* **ICAO Code:** `GMMN`
* **Location:** Nouaceur Province, Casablanca-Settat, Morocco
* **Operator:** ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority)
* **Hub For:** Royal Air Maroc, Air Arabia Maroc
* **Website:** [www.onda.ma](http://www.onda.ma/en/Our-Airports/Casablanca-Mohammed-V-Airport)

---

## Terminals

The airport currently operates two main passenger terminals: Terminal 1 and Terminal 2.

* **Terminal 1:** This is the newer and more modern terminal. It primarily handles flights operated by Royal Air Maroc and its international partners within the Oneworld alliance.
* **Terminal 2:** This terminal serves a wide range of international airlines, including many European legacy carriers and low-cost airlines.

The terminals are located adjacent to each other and are connected by a walkway, making transfers relatively straightforward. Always check your ticket to confirm which terminal your flight departs from.

## Passenger Services and Facilities

Being a large international hub, the airport offers a comprehensive range of services.

### Dining and Shopping
* A wide variety of restaurants, international fast-food chains (like McDonald's and Starbucks), and cafés are available in both terminals, both pre- and post-security.
* Extensive duty-free shopping is available in the departures lounges, offering a large selection of international and local goods.

### Lounges
* Several VIP and business-class lounges are available, including Royal Air Maroc's "Le Zénith" lounge in Terminal 1.

### Financial Services
* Multiple currency exchange counters and ATMs are located throughout both terminals in the arrivals and departures areas.

### Other Services
* **Car Rental:** All major international car rental companies (Hertz, Avis, Sixt, Europcar) have desks in the arrivals hall.
* **Wi-Fi:** Free Wi-Fi is provided throughout the airport.
* **Post Office:** A post office is available on-site.
* **Pharmacy:** A pharmacy is located in the public area of the airport.
* **Prayer Rooms:** Dedicated prayer rooms are available.

## Ground Transportation

### Train
* **This is often the most convenient option.** The airport has a train station located on the basement level (-1) of Terminal 1.
* The **ONCF** national railway company operates hourly trains that connect the airport directly to Casablanca's main train stations, **Casa Voyageurs** and **Casa Port**.
* The journey to Casa Voyageurs takes approximately 35-45 minutes. From there, you can connect to the national rail network or the city's tram system.

### Taxis
* **Grand Taxis** (white in Casablanca) are available 24/7 from the taxi rank outside the arrivals hall.
* Fares to the city center are regulated, but it is crucial to agree on the price with the driver before beginning your journey to avoid overcharging. The trip to the city center takes 45-60 minutes, depending on traffic.

### Buses
* The **CTM** bus company operates coach services from the airport to the city center. This is a budget-friendly option, but the schedule may be less frequent than the train.

### Renting a Car

Renting a car offers complete freedom of movement and is a good option for those who plan to explore the areas surrounding Casablanca.

#### Booking Process
Reliable international rental companies like Avis, Hertz, and Europcar are based at Mohammed V International Airport.

* **Requirements:**
    * You generally need to be at least 21 years old to rent a car and may face a surcharge if you are under 25.
    * A valid driver’s license and a credit card in the driver's name are required.

#### Expected Costs
Costs vary based on car type, rental duration, and season. Manual transmission vehicles are typically more affordable.

* **Economy:** €25 - €40 per day
* **Compact SUV:** €45 - €60 per day
* **Luxury:** €90+ per day

#### Insurance Guide
Most rentals include basic third-party liability insurance, but it often comes with a significant excess (deductible). Carefully review the coverage.

* **Common Options:**
    * **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW):** Reduces or eliminates your financial liability for damage to the rental car.
    * **Personal Accident Insurance (PAI):** Covers medical expenses for the driver and passengers. Check if your personal travel insurance already provides this.
* **Pro Tip:** Some credit cards offer rental car insurance as a benefit. Check with your card provider to see if you are covered, which could save you from buying additional insurance.

#### Driving in Casablanca
Driving in Casablanca is not recommended for the faint-of-heart as traffic can be hectic, and adherence to road rules can be inconsistent.

* Drive on the **right side of the road**.
* Be prepared for heavy traffic, especially during morning and evening rush hours.
* Stay vigilant for pedestrians and other vehicles.

### Intercity Buses

This station connects Casablanca to other cities.

#### Gare Routière Ouled Ziane (Casablanca Main Bus Station)
* **Location:** Rue Youssoufia, Casablanca, Morocco.
* **Description:** This is the main bus station in Casablanca, serving as a central hub for intercity travel to a wide range of destinations across Morocco, including popular cities like Marrakech, Agadir, and Fes. It is a major departure and arrival point for various bus companies.
* **Note:** Casablanca has several bus stops and terminals. Major companies like CTM and Supratours also have their own dedicated stations or a presence at the main bus station. It's recommended to book tickets in advance, especially for popular routes and during peak travel times. Tickets can often be booked online through third-party sites that consolidate multiple bus companies.

### Gare de Casablanca-Voyageurs (Casablanca Train Station)

* **Location:** In the Belvedere neighborhood of central Casablanca. It is well-connected to the rest of the city by taxi and Casablanca Tramway Line 1.
* **Description:** As the largest and most important railway hub in Morocco, Casa-Voyageurs is a major transit point for long-distance travel. The station underwent a major renovation and expansion, with a new, modern building that includes shops, eateries, and a first-class lounge.
* **Key Destinations:** This is the primary station for all long-distance travel in and out of Casablanca. It serves as the southern terminus for the **Al Boraq high-speed train** from Tangier. You can catch trains to all major cities from here, including:
    * Marrakech
    * Rabat
    * Tangier
    * Fez and Meknes
    * Oujda
    * Mohammed V International Airport (CMN)
* **Note:** If you are traveling to or from any major city in Morocco, your train will likely arrive or depart from Casa-Voyageurs.

### Gare de Casa-Port (Casablanca Train Station)

* **Location:** Located in the city center, near the Port of Casablanca.
* **Description:** This is the smaller of Casablanca's two main train stations. While it is not on the main North-South line, it is a convenient stop for travelers staying in the city center due to its proximity to many hotels and attractions.
* **Key Destinations:** Casa-Port primarily serves local and regional trains. You can catch the express "Train Navette Rapide" (TNR) to destinations like:
    * Rabat
    * Kenitra
    * Mohammed V International Airport (CMN)
* **Note:** While you can travel to major cities from Casa-Port, most long-distance routes require a transfer at Casa-Voyageurs.

### Overview of Transportation in Casablanca

The provided documents describe Casablanca's transportation system, with Mohammed V International Airport (CMN) being the primary international gateway, located about 30 km from the city. For travel from the airport to the city, the most convenient option is often the train, which connects directly to Casablanca's main stations. Grand Taxis and CTM buses are also available.

For getting around the city, the Casa Tramway is the most modern and efficient public transport method, featuring two lines and over 70 stations. Local public buses are the most inexpensive option, with single journeys costing 4 dirhams. The city's taxi service is divided into two types: red 'Petit Taxis' for private trips within the city limits and white 'Grand Taxis' which are shared and used for set routes and longer distances.

For intercity travel, the main bus station is the Gare Routière Ouled Ziane, which connects Casablanca to other destinations across Morocco. Renting a car is another option for exploring the surrounding areas, with numerous international agencies at the airport. However, the documents note that driving in Casablanca can be challenging due to hectic traffic.

### Taxis in Casablanca

There are two types of taxis available in Casablanca for getting around the city and for longer day trips.

These are private taxis used for trips within the city.
* **Color:** Red.

* **Color:** White.
* **Type:** A minibus that offers shared rides.

### Casa Tramway

The Casa Tramway is the most modern and efficient method of public transport in the city, with a network of over 70 stations. The trams are considered clean, safe, and comfortable. For the most current information, visit the official Casa Tramway website https://www.casatramway.ma/.

#### Fares and Passes
* **Single Journey:** A single trip costs 6 dirhams.
* **Reloadable Tickets:** Ideal for one or two trips, the disposable ticket costs 2 dirhams, with each journey costing 6 dirhams. It can be used twice before being thrown away.
* **Prepaid Cards:** A popular option for visitors, the card costs 15 dirhams to purchase, and each trip then costs 6 dirhams. You can load credit as you go.
* **Subscription Cards:** For longer stays, this card costs 15 dirhams to purchase, plus a subscription fee for a set period (60 dirhams for a week, 230 for a month) offering unlimited travel.
* **How to Pay:** All tickets and cards can be purchased from vending machines at tramway stations.

#### Key Routes
The tramway currently has two lines, T1 and T2. Note that some key tourist sites like the Old Medina and Hassan II Mosque are not directly connected.
* **Line T1 (Orange):** Travels between Lissasfa Terminus (southwest) and Sidi Moumen Terminus (northeast).
* **Line T2 (Yellow):** Travels between Sidi Bernoussi Terminus (northeast) and Ain Diab Plage Terminus on the oceanfront.

#### Hours of Operation
Trams depart approximately every 15 minutes.
* **Sidi Moumen Terminus (T1):** First departure at 5:45 a.m.; last departure at 7:45 p.m..
* **Lissasfa Terminus (T1):** First departure at 6:15 a.m.; last departure at 8:10 p.m..
* **Sidi Bernoussi Terminus (T2):** First departure at 5:45 a.m.; last departure at 7:05 p.m..
* **Ain Diab Plage Terminus (T2):** First departure at 6:45 a.m.; last departure at 8 p.m..

### Leisure & Recreation

* **Stroll Along The Corniche**
    The Corniche is Casablanca's vibrant seaside promenade, stretching along the Atlantic coast in the **Ain Diab** district. It's a popular spot for locals and tourists to walk, jog, and relax. The promenade is lined with a succession of beaches, swimming pools, cafes, restaurants, and beach clubs. It's the perfect place to enjoy the ocean breeze and experience the city's more modern, leisurely side.

* **Shopping Malls**
    Casablanca offers several modern shopping experiences. Located at the end of the Corniche, **Morocco Mall** is one of the largest in Africa, featuring an indoor aquarium and an IMAX cinema. Other popular options for shopping and dining include **Anfaplace Mall** and **Marina Shopping** center.

* **Marché Central (Central Market)**
    For a lively and authentic local experience, visit the Marché Central. Here you can find fresh seafood, flowers, spices, and produce. It's also a great place to enjoy a fresh fish lunch at one of the small, bustling restaurants within the market.

* **Tamaris Aquaparc**
    A popular water park located just outside the city, offering a variety of slides, pools, and water-based activities for a fun family day out.


### Architectural & Cultural Exploration

* **Visit the Hassan II Mosque**
    This is Casablanca's most iconic landmark and an absolute must-see. It is one of the largest mosques in the world, with a minaret that stands as the second tallest. Its stunning location on the edge of the Atlantic Ocean is breathtaking. A significant portion of the mosque's floor is made of glass, allowing worshippers to see the sea below. Unlike most mosques in Morocco, it is open to non-Muslim visitors through guided tours, which showcase the exquisite Moroccan craftsmanship of its interiors.

* **Explore the Art Deco District (Ville Art-Déco)**
    Take a walk through the city center to discover Casablanca's unique architectural heritage. The area around **Mohammed V Square** is filled with buildings from the French Protectorate era, designed in the Mauresque (Moorish revival) and Art Deco styles. Key buildings include the Grande Poste (Main Post Office), the Wilaya (City Hall), and the Palace of Justice.

* **Wander Through the Old Medina**
    While smaller and less tourist-oriented than those in Fes or Marrakech, Casablanca's Old Medina offers an authentic glimpse into the city's past. Navigate its narrow, winding streets to see the historic city walls and explore small, traditional souks selling leather goods, spices, and handmade crafts.

* **Discover the Habous Quarter (New Medina of Casablanca)**
    Built by the French in the 1930s, the Habous Quarter was designed to blend traditional Moroccan architecture with modern urban planning. The result is a charming and orderly district with clean streets, arched walkways, and neat souks. It's an ideal place to shop for high-quality handicrafts, books, and traditional clothing in a more relaxed environment. Don't miss the impressive **Mahkama Du Pacha**, a stunning example of Hispano-Moorish architecture that formerly served as a courthouse.

* **Visit Religious Landmarks**
    Casablanca is home to significant religious sites for different faiths. These include the modern **Church of Notre Dame of Lourdes**, the historical **The Church Of The Sacred Heart**, and the active synagogue, **Temple Beth-El**.

* **See the Royal Palace of Casablanca**
    While the palace is not open to the public, the exterior of this grand royal residence is an impressive sight and showcases opulent Moroccan design.

* **Al saoud foundation
The Al saoud foundation Casablanca, a treasure dedicated to Moroccan and Islamic culture .perfect for history enthusiasts, this unique library promises you an enriching immersion.

### Historic Sites & Viewpoints

* **Hassan II Mosque**
    The city's most breathtaking landmark, the Hassan II Mosque is one of the largest in the world. Its minaret dominates the skyline, and its location on the edge of the Atlantic is stunning. * **Old Medina**

    The coastal promenade of Ain Diab serves as a major city landmark, offering sweeping views of the Atlantic. It is lined with beaches, cafes, and restaurants, making it a focal point for leisure and recreation. The **El Hank Lighthouse**, at its western end, stands as a historic maritime beacon.

## Nightlife & Entertainment in Casablanca

As Morocco's most modern and cosmopolitan city, Casablanca offers a vibrant and sophisticated nightlife scene that stands apart from the more traditional entertainment found in other parts of the country. The city's entertainment is concentrated in its chic, modern districts, offering everything from high-energy nightclubs to refined cocktail bars.

---

### The Corniche (Ain Diab)

The heart of Casablanca's nightlife is the coastal strip known as the Corniche, particularly in the Ain Diab neighborhood. This is where the city comes to party. The area is packed with:

* **Nightclubs:** Large, modern clubs featuring international DJs and playing a mix of electronic, pop, and Arabic music.
* **Beach Clubs:** Stylish venues that transition from daytime seaside lounges to lively evening hotspots.
* **Restaurants & Bars:** A wide array of seaside restaurants and bars offering dinner, drinks, and ocean views.

The atmosphere here is energetic and glamorous, attracting a crowd ready to dance the night away.

For a more relaxed yet trendy evening, the central neighborhoods of Gauthier and Maarif are key destinations. These areas are known for their sophisticated and cosmopolitan vibe. Here you will find:

* **Cocktail Bars & Lounges:** Chic, well-designed bars perfect for a more intimate and conversation-focused evening.
* **Trendy Restaurants:** A high concentration of the city's best international and modern Moroccan restaurants.
* **Wine Bars & Gastropubs:** Venues with a more European feel, offering a wide selection of drinks and upscale bar food.

This part of the city caters to a stylish crowd looking for a refined night out.

### City Center (Centre Ville)

The downtown area offers more traditional nightlife options. Many of the larger hotels in the Centre Ville have their own established bars and lounges, which are reliable spots for a classic cocktail or a quiet drink. You can also find a variety of brasseries and pubs with a more classic, international feel.

## A Guide to Souks and Shopping in Casablanca

Shopping in Casablanca offers a tale of two cities: the traditional, bustling souks of its historic medinas and the chic, modern boutiques and sprawling malls of its contemporary districts. This guide covers the key areas to explore for an authentic market experience or a modern retail spree.

---

### Traditional Souks & Markets

#### The Old Medina
The souks within Casablanca's Old Medina offer a raw and authentic market experience. While smaller and less tourist-focused than the markets of Marrakech or Fes, these labyrinthine alleys are packed with stalls selling everyday goods, leather products, traditional clothing, and spices. It's a place to witness daily life and find unique items in a bustling, historic setting.

#### The Habous Quarter (New Medina)
For a more relaxed and orderly traditional shopping experience, the Habous Quarter is the ideal destination. Built by the French in the 1930s, it was designed to be a "new medina" with clean, arched walkways and well-organized shops. It's renowned for its high-quality, handcrafted goods.

**Key Features:**
* Specializes in traditional Moroccan crafts, including custom-made kaftans, fine leather goods, and intricate brass and copperware.
* Home to excellent olive and spice markets, as well as renowned bakeries
* The atmosphere is calmer than the Old Medina, making it a more comfortable place to browse for souvenirs and high-quality items.

#### The Central Market (Marché Central)
Located in the city center, the Marché Central is a vibrant, open-air market that offers a feast for the senses. It's the city's primary destination for fresh produce.

**Key Features:**
* A bustling flower market at its entrance.
* The main hall is dedicated to the fresh catch of the day, with an incredible variety of fish and seafood.
* Features numerous small, open-air restaurants where you can **choose your fish and have it grilled or fried on the spot**.

---

### Modern Shopping

#### The Maarif District
The Maarif district, located near the iconic Twin Center, is one of Casablanca's most dynamic modern shopping areas. The streets are lined with a mix of major international brands , local Moroccan boutiques, and trendy shops. It's a vibrant neighborhood that reflects the city's cosmopolitan energy.


#### Shopping Malls
Casablanca is home to several large, modern shopping centers that offer a world-class retail experience.
* **Morocco Mall:** One of the largest malls in Africa, it houses hundreds of international brands, a large supermarket, an indoor aquarium, and an IMAX cinema.
* **Anfaplace Mall & Marina Shopping:** These malls also offer a wide selection of international stores, restaurants, and cafes, often with views of the ocean.

### Regional Specialties & Signature Dishes

#### Grilled Seafood & Fried Fish
Thanks to its extensive coastline, Casablanca excels in fresh seafood. The most authentic experience is found at the **Marché Central (Central Market)**, where you can choose from the day's catch—including calamari, shrimp, oysters, and a wide variety of fish—and have it fried or grilled on the spot. It's often served simply with a side of fresh Moroccan salads, bread, and zesty dipping sauces.

#### Tagine
The quintessential Moroccan slow-cooked stew is a staple in Casablanca. While you can find all the classic variations like lamb with prunes or chicken with preserved lemons, the coastal influence makes **Fish Tagine (Tagine de Poisson)** a popular local choice. It typically features firm white fish cooked with tomatoes, potatoes, olives, and a zesty 'chermoula' marinade.

#### Couscous
A national dish, couscous consists of fine, steamed semolina granules served with a rich stew of vegetables and meat (typically lamb, beef, or chicken). Traditionally eaten on Fridays, you'll find it on almost every menu, often as a daily special.

#### Pastilla (B'stilla)
This elaborate and festive pie offers a unique blend of sweet and savory flavors. In Casablanca, alongside the classic pigeon or chicken versions, you can often find a delicious **Seafood Pastilla (Pastilla aux Fruits de Mer)**. This variety is filled with shrimp, calamari, and fish, mixed with vermicelli noodles and a savory, slightly spicy 'chermoula' sauce, all wrapped in flaky 'warqa' pastry.

#### Harira
The traditional Moroccan soup, a hearty and flavorful blend of tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas, and fresh herbs. It's a staple in local eateries and is especially popular in the evenings, often served with dates and a sweet pastry called 'chebakia'.

#### Brochettes (Kebabs)
Grilled meat skewers are a popular and delicious staple. You can find them in both street-side stalls and restaurants, with options ranging from lamb, beef, and chicken to spicy 'merguez' sausage, all grilled over charcoal for a smoky flavor.

### Early History

 The current site of Casablanca was inhabited by humans during the Paleolithic era. The origins of the city are not exactly known, but it seems that the city of Anfa was once located in the same place as it is today.Archaeological discoveries at Sidi Abderrahman (the south exit of Casablanca) attest to the settlement of the site since prehistoric times. It appears that Anfa was inhabited by amazigh  fishermen from the earliest antiquity, a time when the place served as a stopover for Phoenician ships en route to the Purpurarius Islands off Essaouira. By 744 AD, the city had become a significant part of the Berber kingdom of Barghawata. The historian Leo Africanus described Anfa as the most "prosperous city on the Atlantic Coast because of its fertile land."

In 1068, the Almoravids conquered the region, ending the independence of the Barghawata. Following this, Arab tribes of Hilal and Sulaym descent settled in the area, leading to widespread Arabization through intermingling with the local Berber population. Under the Merinid dynasty in the 14th century, Anfa's importance as a port grew. However, the Merinids were eventually ousted by a popular revolt in 1465.

### The French Protectorate and Economic Boom

The 19th century brought increased trade with Europe, and Casablanca's population began to grow. Its modern transformation began in the early 20th century. In July 1907, the French operated a small 'decauville' train for the construction works of the port (a simple dock at the time). The track runs through the nearby cemetery of the Sidi Beliout sanctuary, disturbing the serenity of the place. A popular riot erupts against the European workers, of whom 7 are killed. France sends troops to restore order, as the Chaouia tribes have also attacked the city and looted the Mellah (Jewish quarter).
 
With the official establishment of the **French Protectorate** in 1912, Casablanca was designated as the main economic hub of French Morocco. The French architect and urban planner **Henri Prost** was commissioned to design the modern city. He laid out a new city center (the Ville Nouvelle) outside the walls of the old medina, with wide boulevards, grand public squares like Place Mohammed V, and impressive Mauresque and Art Deco style buildings.

A modern port was constructed, and the city experienced an explosive economic and demographic boom, attracting Moroccans from across the country as well as European expatriates. It quickly became the country's largest and most important city.

### Overview of Casablanca's History

The provided documents trace the history of Casablanca from its ancient origins as a Berber settlement named Anfa in the seventh century BC. It served as a port for Phoenicians and Romans and grew in importance under various dynasties. In the 15th century, Anfa became a harbor for pirates, which led to its destruction by the Portuguese in 1468. The Portuguese rebuilt a settlement on the ruins called Casa Branca, which was later reconstructed between 1756 and 1790 by Sultan Mohammed ben Abdallah, who gave it the Arabic name ad-Dār al-Bayḍāʼ.

The city's modern era began with the French Protectorate in 1912, under which Casablanca was developed into the primary economic hub of French Morocco. Architect Henri Prost was commissioned to design a new city center, which sparked an economic and demographic boom. During World War II, Casablanca was a key strategic site, hosting the Casablanca Conference in 1943 where Allied leaders agreed to the "unconditional surrender" policy for the Axis powers.

After Moroccan independence in 1956, Casablanca solidified its role as the nation's economic and industrial capital. Today, it is the largest city in the Maghreb and a major financial center in Africa, remaining the engine of the Moroccan economy.

### Portuguese Conquest and Rebuilding

In the early 15th century, Anfa re-emerged as an independent state and became a known safe harbor for pirates and privateers. This drew the attention of the Portuguese, who bombarded and destroyed the town in 1468. The Portuguese rebuilt a settlement on the ruins, naming it Casa Branca, meaning "White House."

The city was later rebuilt between 1756 and 1790 by Sultan Mohammed ben Abdallah, the grandson of Moulay Ismail. The town was given the name ad-Dār al-Bayḍāʼ (الدار البيضاء), which is the Arabic translation of the Portuguese "Casa Branca."

### 19th Century Growth

During the 19th century, Casablanca's population and importance grew significantly. It became a major supplier of wool to the booming textile industry in Great Britain, which led to a substantial increase in shipping traffic. By the 1860s, the city had around 5,000 residents, a number that doubled to 10,000 by the late 1880s. On the eve of the French conquest in 1906, Casablanca was a modestly sized but growing port with a population of about 12,000.

### World War II and the Casablanca Conference

Casablanca played a significant strategic role during World War II. It was a key landing site for American forces during **Operation Torch** in November 1942.

In January 1943, it hosted the famous **Casablanca Conference**, a top-secret meeting between U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill, and French Generals Charles de Gaulle and Henri Giraud. It was at this conference that the Allied leaders agreed on the strategy of demanding an "unconditional surrender" from the Axis powers.

### Casablanca Today

After Morocco gained its independence in 1956, Casablanca maintained its status as the nation's economic and industrial capital. The city continued to expand rapidly throughout the latter half of the 20th century.

Today, Casablanca is the largest city in the Maghreb and one of the most important financial centers in Africa. While Rabat is the political capital, Casablanca is the undisputed engine of the Moroccan economy, home to the nation's primary port, major corporate headquarters, and a thriving stock exchange. It stands as a symbol of modern Morocco's dynamism and global integration.

### Types of Accommodation

#### Luxury & Business Hotels
As Morocco's primary commercial hub, Casablanca excels in providing high-end hotels geared towards business and leisure travelers. These are typically found in the city center and the upscale Anfa neighborhood, offering extensive amenities such as conference facilities, fine dining restaurants, swimming pools, and spas.

#### Apartment Hotels (Aparthotels)
Ideal for longer stays or travelers desiring more independence, apartment hotels offer a blend of hotel services with the convenience of a private apartment. Rooms are usually equipped with kitchenettes or full kitchens, living areas, and amenities like housekeeping and reception services.

#### Apartments
Renting a private apartment is a popular option for families, groups, or those wanting to experience the city like a local. This choice offers the most space and flexibility, with properties available in both modern residential neighborhoods and more traditional areas.

#### Hostels and Guesthouses
Budget travelers and backpackers will find a selection of modern hostels in Casablanca, offering both dormitory-style and private rooms. While traditional Riads are less common here than in Marrakech or Fes, there are some charming guesthouses that provide a more intimate and authentic Moroccan experience, often located in or near the Old Medina.
