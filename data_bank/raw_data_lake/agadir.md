# Agadir–Al Massira Airport (AGA)

## Overview

Agadir–Al Massira Airport (`IATA: AGA`, `ICAO: GMAD`) is the primary international airport serving the city of Agadir and the surrounding Souss-Massa region of southwestern Morocco. It is a key gateway for tourists visiting the popular coastal resorts and plays a vital role in the region's economy. The airport is operated by ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority) and features a modern, single-terminal design capable of handling millions of passengers annually.

## Quick Facts

* **Official Name:** Agadir–Al Massira Airport
* **IATA Code:** `AGA`
* **ICAO Code:** `GMAD`
* **Location:** Approximately 25 km (16 miles) southeast of Agadir's city center.
* **Operator:** ONDA (Moroccan Airports Authority)
* **Runways:** One primary runway (09/27)
* **Website:** [www.onda.ma](http://www.onda.ma/en/Our-Airports/Agadir-Al-Massira-Airport)

## Terminals

The airport consists of **one main passenger terminal** which handles both domestic and international flights. The terminal is a modern, spacious building designed to accommodate the significant seasonal tourist traffic.

* **First Floor:** Departures hall, check-in desks, security screening, passport control, boarding gates, and a large duty-free shopping area.

### Renting a Car

Renting a car in Agadir offers flexibility and freedom to explore the city and surrounding regions like Paradise Valley and Taghazout at your own pace, without being tied to tour schedules or taxi fares.

#### Booking Process
It is highly recommended to book your car online in advance, especially during peak tourist seasons (e.g., July and August), to ensure availability and secure better rates. Rental agencies are located at **Agadir-Al Massira Airport (AGA)** and within the city center. Both major international and local Moroccan rental agencies are available, with local companies often offering competitive rates.

* **Requirements:**
    * A valid driving license from your country of residence.
    * An International Driving Permit (IDP) may be required by some companies. It is essential to verify this requirement before booking.

#### Expected Costs
Costs vary based on car type, rental duration, and season. Manual transmission vehicles are typically more affordable than automatic ones.

* **Economy (e.g., Renault Clio):** €20 - €30 per day
* **Compact SUV:** €35 - €50 per day
* **Luxury:** €80+ per day

#### Insurance Guide
Most rentals include basic third-party liability insurance, but it often comes with a significant excess (deductible). Carefully review the coverage.

* **Common Options:**
    * **Collision Damage Waiver (CDW):** Reduces or eliminates your financial liability for damage to the rental car in the event of an accident.
    * **Personal Accident Insurance (PAI):** Covers medical expenses for the driver and passengers. Check if your personal travel insurance already provides this coverage before purchasing.
* **Pro Tip:** Some credit cards offer rental car insurance as a benefit. Check with your card provider to see if you are covered, which could save you from buying additional insurance.

#### Driving in Agadir
Driving in Agadir is generally manageable, with roads in good condition.

* Drive on the **right side of the road**.
* On roundabouts, give way to traffic that is already circulating.
* Strictly adhere to posted speed limits, as speed cameras are common.
* Stay vigilant for pedestrians, who may cross the road unexpectedly.

### Taxis in Agadir

For trips within the city using a Petit Taxi, always check that the driver will use the meter before starting your journey to avoid overpaying.


#### Petit Taxi (Small Taxi)
These are the standard taxis for travel within the Agadir city limits.
* **Color:** Red
* **Scope:** Legally authorized for trips only within the city.
* **Pricing:** All are equipped with a taxi meter which is mandatory for calculating the fare. The meter starts at a base fare of around 6 MAD, with most trips costing up to 50 MAD.
* **Passengers:** Up to three, plus a child.

#### Grand Taxi (Large Taxi)
These taxis are used for longer distances, such as trips to the airport, nearby towns, or areas outside the city center.
* **Color:** Typically white or blue.
* **Scope:** Authorized for longer, inter-city, and suburban trips.
* **Pricing:** They do not use meters. The fare **is fixed**.
* **Passengers:** Up to seven.

### Gardens & Parks

* **Jardin d'Olhão (Olhao Garden)**
    A tranquil public garden built to commemorate the twinning agreement between Agadir and Olhão, Portugal. It features distinct Portuguese-style architecture and water features and is also home to the Agadir Memorial Museum.

    A large zoological and botanical park that is home to over 300 Nile crocodiles. The park also features a rich botanical garden, including a "Blue Garden" with a collection of rare succulents and other exotic plants.

 After having undergone various developments during its existence, notably the integration of a skatepark and a set of sports equipment in the upper part of the garden in 2008, the Ibn Zaydoun Garden today benefits from a total requalification thanks to the Environmental Preservation & Development of green spaces component of the Agadir PDU 2020-2024.

### Historic Sites

* **Agadir Oufella (The Kasbah)**
    The ruins of the city's original 16th-century fortress, located on a hill overlooking the modern city and port. Little remained of the structure after the 1960 earthquake, but the site has been renovations ever since. The site offers unparalleled panoramic views and a tangible connection to Agadir's pre-earthquake history. The hill is famous for its large inscription: "God, Country, King." which is the country's motto.

### Museums

* **Musée du Patrimoine Amazigh (Museum of Amazigh Culture)**
    This museum is dedicated to preserving and showcasing the rich cultural heritage of the Amazigh (Berber) people of the Souss-Massa region. The collection features traditional jewelry, carpets, textiles, tools, and manuscripts, and is particularly known for its extensive collection of Amazigh silver jewelry.

* **Agadir Memorial Museum (Musée Mémorial d'Agadir)**
    Located within the Jardin d'Olhão, this small museum is dedicated to the memory of the devastating 1960 earthquake that destroyed the old city. It features photographs and newspaper clippings from the era, commemorating this pivotal event in Agadir's history.

* **Universe of Argan Museum**
    An experiential museum that tells the story of the endemic Argan tree. It details the traditional methods of producing Argan oil and showcases its importance in local culture and cuisine.

* **Le Centre national du patrimoine rupestre (CNPR)**
    The National Center for Rock Art Heritage (CNPR) in Agadir is an institution established in 1994 with the mission of documenting, studying, and protecting rock art in Morocco.

## A Guide to Souks and Markets in Agadir

Exploring the local markets, or souks, is an essential part of experiencing the culture and daily life of Agadir. Here's a guide to the two most important markets in the city.

---

### Souk El Had d'Agadir

Souk El Had is the largest urban market in the region and one of the most extensive in Africa. Enclosed by high walls, this bustling marketplace is the commercial heart of Agadir for both locals and visitors. It is a vibrant and sprawling complex offering a sensory overload of sights, sounds, and smells.



**Key Features:**
* Contains over **6,000 individual stalls** and shops.
* Organized into distinct sections for easier navigation, including areas for spices, leather goods, traditional crafts (tagines, lamps), clothing, furniture, and fresh produce.
* The entire souk is enclosed within a fortified wall with multiple numbered gates, which serve as key landmarks for orientation.

**Visitor Tips:**
* **Bargaining is expected** and is a fundamental part of the shopping experience.
* Use the **gate numbers** (e.g., Gate 9, Gate 10) to remember your entry point and navigate the vast area.
* The souk is **closed on Mondays** for cleaning.

---

### Central Fish Market (Marché Central au Poisson)

Located near the city's port and marina, the Central Fish Market is a lively hub dedicated to seafood. It showcases the daily catch from Agadir's fishing fleet and is renowned for its authenticity and the freshness of its products.



**Key Features:**
* Offers a wide variety of fresh fish and seafood sold directly by fishermen.
* Features numerous small, open-air restaurants within the market where you can **choose your fish and have it grilled on the spot**.
* The atmosphere is most vibrant in the morning as boats come in and wholesale trading takes place.

**Visitor Tips:**
* For the best selection and to witness the market at its liveliest, **visit in the morning**.
* Wear closed-toe shoes as the floors can be wet.
* Prices for grilling are usually per kilogram and may include sides like bread, salad, and sauces.

### Regional Specialties & Signature Dishes

#### Grilled Seafood (Poissons Grillés)
As a major Atlantic port, Agadir's most iconic meal is fresh seafood grilled to perfection. Often seasoned simply with salt, cumin, and lemon to highlight the freshness of the catch, this is best experienced at the Central Fish Market near the port, where you can select your fish (sardines, calamari, shrimp, sea bream) and have it cooked on the spot.

#### Amlou
A delicious and energizing spread that is a true specialty of the Souss region. It is a thick, brown paste made from roasted almonds, pure culinary Argan oil, and honey. It's typically served for breakfast or as a snack and used as a dip for bread.

#### Tagine
This slow-cooked stew is a national dish, and in Agadir, the **Fish Tagine (Tagine de Poisson)** is a local specialty. This version often features firm local fish like monkfish (lotte) or conger eel, cooked with tomatoes, potatoes, peppers, and a flavorful 'chermoula' marinade. You will also find classic variations with lamb or chicken, often simmered with preserved lemon and olives.

#### Culinary Argan Oil (Huile d'Argan Alimentaire)
The Argan tree is endemic to the region around Agadir, making its culinary oil a fundamental local ingredient. It has a distinct, nutty flavor from roasting the kernels and is used as a finishing oil drizzled over salads and tagines, or simply as a dip for bread.

#### Tafarnout Bread
A large, round, and somewhat flat traditional Amazigh bread known for its chewy texture and rustic flavor. It is traditionally baked on a bed of hot stones or in a communal clay oven and is the perfect vessel for dipping in tagine, Argan oil, or Amlou.

#### Tagoula
A hearty and simple Amazigh porridge made from barley semolina. It is considered a staple of Amazigh cuisine in the region and is typically served in a large communal dish with a well created in the center, which is then filled with Argan oil, Amlou, or smen (fermented butter) for dipping.

#### Sardines
Agadir has one of the most important sardine ports in the Atlantic. Grilled sardines marinated in a zesty chermoula sauce are a must-try local specialty.

#### Harira
A hearty, traditional Moroccan soup made with lentils, chickpeas, and tomatoes. It is often served with a soft-boiled egg, dates, and a sweet chebakia cookie.

#### Pastilla
A savory-sweet pie with flaky pastry, filled with spiced pigeon or chicken, almonds, and cinnamon.

### Overview of Agadir's Cuisine

These documents describe the local cuisine of Agadir and the surrounding Souss region, which is heavily influenced by its location as a major Atlantic port and its Amazigh heritage. The files highlight iconic regional specialties such as Amlou, a spread made from roasted almonds, Argan oil, and honey, and culinary Argan oil, which is endemic to the area.

### Early History and Portuguese Occupation

The first known map indicating Agadir dates to 1325, naming a place called **Porto Mesegina** after a local Amazigh tribe. The name Agadir first appeared in records around 1510.

In 1505, Portuguese nobleman **João Lopes de Sequeira** established a trading post and a wooden castle at the foot of the hill, an area still known as **Founti** (from the Portuguese word for "fountain"). This colony was named **Santa Cruz do Cabo do Gué**. However, growing local hostility, led by the nascent **Sa'di dynasty**, culminated in a siege. In 1541, the Sa'di Sultan **Mohammed ash-Sheikh** captured the fortress, ending the Portuguese presence and forcing them to abandon most of their Moroccan territories.

### The French Protectorate and Modern Development

French troops landed in Agadir in 1913. Under the French protectorate, the city saw its first modern development. A port was constructed, and new districts like the old **Talborjt** and **Yahchech** were built. Around 1930, Agadir became an important stop for the French airmail service **Aéropostale**, frequented by famous aviators like Saint-Exupéry. A modern city center began to take shape based on the plans of urban planners Henri Prost and Michel Ecochard.

By the 1950s, with the opening of a new commercial port, the city grew rapidly, driven by fishing, canning, agriculture, and mining. It also began to establish itself as a tourist destination.

### 17th Century 

After the Sa'di victory, Sultan Abdallah al-Ghalib built a new fortress, or kasbah, on the hilltop, known as Agadir N'Ighir. During the 17th century, Agadir became an important harbor for trade with Europe, primarily dealing in sugar, wax, copper, and hides.
 
However, the city's fortunes turned dramatically. In 1731, it was completely destroyed by an earthquake. Following this, Sultan Mohammed ben Abdallah ordered the port to be closed in 1760, favoring the newly established port of Essaouira as a way to punish the Souss region for rebelling. This led to a long period of decline, and by 1789, Agadir was described as a ghost town.


### 19th Century and European Influence

The port was reopened in 1881 by Sultan **Hassan I** to supply his military expeditions in the south. This reopening also attracted European interest.

In 1911, Germany sent the gunboat **SMS Panther** to the Bay of Agadir on the pretext of protecting German commercial interests. This move was intended to challenge French influence in Morocco and triggered the **Agadir Crisis**, a major international incident that brought Europe to the brink of war. The crisis was resolved with the signing of a Franco-German treaty, which gave France a free hand to establish its protectorate over Morocco.

### The 1960 Earthquake and Rebirth

On February 29, 1960, at 11:45 PM, a catastrophic earthquake with a magnitude of 5.7 struck Agadir. Lasting only 15 seconds, it completely destroyed the old city, including the historic Kasbah, and killed an estimated 15,000 people—more than a third of the city's population of 40,000.

The current city was rebuilt 1.6 kilometers to the south, with modern, earthquake-resistant design principles, led by a team of Moroccan and international architects.

### Agadir Today

From the ruins of the 1960 earthquake, Agadir has risen to become a major Moroccan city with a population of over half a million. It is a vital center for tourism, known for its beach stretching over 10 km and a climate that offers 340 days of sunshine a year. It is also the most important fishing port in the country and a key hub for the export of citrus fruits and vegetables from the fertile Souss Valley.

### Types of Accommodation

#### Resorts and Hotels
This is the most popular type of accommodation, especially along the beach. You'll find many large resorts, often with all-inclusive options, multiple swimming pools, spas, and entertainment. These are ideal for families, couples, and those looking for a relaxing, hassle-free vacation.

#### Apartment Hotels (Aparthotels)

#### Apartments and Villas
For travelers who prefer more space and a home-like experience, renting an apartment or a villa is a great option. This is especially good for families or groups and allows you to cook your own meals and live like a local.

#### Hostels and Guesthouses
If you're a backpacker or on a tighter budget, hostels are a fantastic choice. They offer a social atmosphere and are a great way to meet other travelers. Guesthouses, or "riads" in a more traditional Moroccan context, offer a more intimate and authentic experience, often located in local neighborhoods.

#### Surf Camps
Agadir and the surrounding areas, particularly Taghazout and Anza, are known for their surfing scene. Surf camps offer a complete package of accommodation, surf lessons, and often meals, making them perfect for those who want to focus on surfing and meet like-minded people.
