The History of taroudant

If there is disagreement among historians about the date of the founding of the city of Taroudant, they all agree that the foundation of the city is attributed to the emirs of the Hachtouka and Jazoula tribes, and recognize that Taroudant was a center of political and commercial activity for the Soussi tribes.Taroudant, the capital of the Shiite Bajjalis kingdom in the 11th century, the first capital of the Saadians in the 16th century, a refuge for rebel princes, coveted by the southern tribes, destroyed in 1306 by the Merinids, is one of the oldest cities in Morocco and holds a prominent place in the history of the country. It played a very important role in the history of Souss and Morocco in general, both during the pre-Islamic period and through the various stages of Muslim Maghreb history.
At the start of the sixteenth century, Taroudant experienced a revival in its economic significance and strategic role due to the rise of the Saadian dynasty. Mohammed Sheikh was instrumental in this resurgence, as he constructed several monuments that shaped the city's architectural identity; he restored and strengthened the city walls, built the kasbah, the grand mosque, and an educational institution. Additionally, the outskirts of Taroudant saw the establishment of notable sugar factories, contributing to its status as a key economic and cultural center.
As the influence of the Saadian state waned, the city became a site of local conflicts until the Alawite state was established, marked by Moulay Rashid's entry in 1669-1670. After the death of Moulay Ismail, the city faced instability until Sidi Mohammed Ben Abdellah received allegiance, during which the entire Sous region was beyond central control. The efforts of Moulay Hassan I were significant in reestablishing the state's authority over this area, including Taroudant.
The French occupation left many landmarks that commemorate its presence in the city in the early 20th century, including the Paul Chatinier Hospital (currently Al-Mokhtar Souissi) and the Public Learning School (Ibn Souleiman Al-Roudani High School) within the old city. They were established together at the beginning of the second decade of the 20th century in 1922. The French also created a European city outside the walls to the northeast between the Kasbah wall and the observation point area. It includes several administrative facilities from the French colonization such as the post office, which played an important role in telecommunication and securing the administrative postal system, the police headquarters, the management of forest and woodland properties, and the French school (Al-Mansour Al-Thahabi) in 1942.
The city spent difficult years under the influence of French civil affairs officers, who represented the French protectorate, which led its educated youth to engage in resistance.
In 1959, King Mohammed V laid the foundation stone for the construction of the Islamic Institute. In 1981, King Hassan II, as part of a policy to bring the administration closer to citizens, reinstated Taroudant as a distinguished base for Souss, by issuing an order to make this city the capital of the Taroudant region, according to royal decree No. 2-81-854 dated 2 Safar 1402, corresponding to December 18, 1981.
