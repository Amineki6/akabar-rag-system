### 17th Century 

After the Sa'di victory, Sultan Abdallah al-Ghalib built a new fortress, or kasbah, on the hilltop, known as Agadir N'Ighir. During the 17th century, Agadir became an important harbor for trade with Europe, primarily dealing in sugar, wax, copper, and hides.
 
However, the city's fortunes turned dramatically. In 1731, it was completely destroyed by an earthquake. Following this, Sultan Mohammed ben Abdallah ordered the port to be closed in 1760, favoring the newly established port of Essaouira as a way to punish the Souss region for rebelling. This led to a long period of decline, and by 1789, Agadir was described as a ghost town.


### 19th Century and European Influence

The port was reopened in 1881 by Sultan **Hassan I** to supply his military expeditions in the south. This reopening also attracted European interest.

In 1911, Germany sent the gunboat **SMS Panther** to the Bay of Agadir on the pretext of protecting German commercial interests. This move was intended to challenge French influence in Morocco and triggered the **Agadir Crisis**, a major international incident that brought Europe to the brink of war. The crisis was resolved with the signing of a Franco-German treaty, which gave France a free hand to establish its protectorate over Morocco.
