Chefchaouen, founded in 1471, is a city with a rich history, particularly shaped by its role as a fortress against Portuguese incursions and as a refuge for people from Andalusia. While its famous blue-painted streets are its most recognizable feature, the city's history is embedded in several key sites:

### Within the Medina
* **The Kasbah:** This fortified castle is the historical heart of Chefchaouen. It was the original fortress built by Moulay Ali Ben Rachid to defend the city. Today, it houses a beautiful Andalusian garden, an ethnographic museum, and an art gallery. Visitors can also climb the towers for panoramic views of the medina and the surrounding Rif Mountains.
* **Plaza Uta el-Hammam:** This main square is the central hub of the old city and has been a gathering place for centuries. It is surrounded by the Kasbah and the Grand Mosque, and its many cafes and restaurants offer a perfect spot to soak in the historical atmosphere.
* **The Grand Mosque (Grande Mosquée):** Located in Plaza Uta el-Hammam, this 15th-century mosque is one of the city's most important religious buildings. It is notable for its unique octagonal minaret, a feature that is rare in Morocco.

### Outside the Medina
* **The Spanish Mosque (Jemaa Bouzafar):** Perched on a hill above the city, this mosque was built in the 1920s by the Spanish. While it is now abandoned, it is a very popular spot for travelers, who make the short hike up to catch a stunning sunset and a breathtaking view of the blue medina.
* **Ras El Maa:** This natural spring is a historical landmark and a focal point of the local community. It is where the city's water supply begins, and for centuries, locals have gathered here to socialize and wash their clothes.
