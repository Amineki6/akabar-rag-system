Chefchaouen's early history is defined by its strategic location and its role as a sanctuary for refugees. The city's founding is a pivotal moment that set the stage for the unique cultural blend it's known for today.

### The Founding of Chefchaouen

The city was founded in **1471** by **Moulay Ali ibn Rachid al-Alami**, a noble descendant of the Prophet Muhammad. His primary motivation was to build a fortress, or **kasbah**, to defend the area against the encroaching Portuguese invaders who were expanding their influence along the northern coast of Morocco. The location, nestled in a valley of the Rif Mountains, was chosen for its natural defensive qualities, making it an ideal stronghold.

### A Refuge from Andalusia

Shortly after its founding, the city began to grow significantly with the arrival of a large influx of Muslim and Jewish refugees from Andalusia (modern-day Spain). These communities were fleeing the Spanish Reconquista and the persecution of the Spanish Inquisition in the late 15th century. They brought with them their culture, traditions, and architectural styles, which had a profound and lasting impact on Chefchaouen.

### The Influence of Andalusian Culture

The influence of these Andalusian refugees is still visible throughout the city today. The medina's winding, narrow streets, the architectural design of the houses with their courtyards, and the very act of painting the houses with shades of blue and white are all believed to have been introduced by these newcomers. The influx of Andalusians transformed Chefchaouen from a simple military outpost into a prosperous city with a vibrant, multicultural identity.

On December 19th, the first voyageurs arrived: the French explorer Charles Foucauld, a rabbi, and the English journalist Walter Harris, a resident of the Rif, as William Summers, an American missionary and a foreigner.

For centuries, Chefchaouen remained a relatively isolated and "holy" city, largely closed to foreigners until the Spanish occupation in the 20th century. Its early history as a fortified sanctuary for those in exile is the foundation of the peaceful and welcoming atmosphere that defines the city today.
