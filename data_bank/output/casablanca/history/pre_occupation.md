### Portuguese Conquest and Rebuilding

In the early 15th century, Anfa re-emerged as an independent state and became a known safe harbor for pirates and privateers. This drew the attention of the Portuguese, who bombarded and destroyed the town in 1468. The Portuguese rebuilt a settlement on the ruins, naming it Casa Branca, meaning "White House."

The city was later rebuilt between 1756 and 1790 by Sultan Mohammed ben Abdallah, the grandson of Moulay Ismail. The town was given the name ad-Dār al-Bayḍāʼ (الدار البيضاء), which is the Arabic translation of the Portuguese "Casa Branca."

### 19th Century Growth

During the 19th century, Casablanca's population and importance grew significantly. It became a major supplier of wool to the booming textile industry in Great Britain, which led to a substantial increase in shipping traffic. By the 1860s, the city had around 5,000 residents, a number that doubled to 10,000 by the late 1880s. On the eve of the French conquest in 1906, Casablanca was a modestly sized but growing port with a population of about 12,000.
