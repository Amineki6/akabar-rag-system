### Overview of fez's Attractions

Fez is one of Morocco's oldest and most historically significant cities, widely regarded as the country's cultural and spiritual heart. Its main draw is the labyrinthine medina, a UNESCO World Heritage site, which offers an immersive journey back in time.
 
**Fassi Zellige** is one of the oldest and most famous types of traditional mosaic in Morocco, considered a jewel of Moroccan and Islamic architectural art. It is distinguished by its unique technique, vibrant colors, and complex geometric designs, and is an integral part of the cultural heritage of the city of Fez, which is celebrated as the capital of this art form.

### History and Origins of Fassi Zellige

The art of zellige dates back to the Middle Ages and flourished in Morocco and Andalusia. The craft reached its peak in the city of Fez during the Marinid dynasty in the 13th and 14th centuries, where it was used to adorn palaces, mosques, and madrasas (schools). Fassi artisans excelled in creating intricate and harmonious geometric patterns, which made Fassi zellige globally renowned for its high quality and extreme precision.

### Characteristics of Fassi Zellige

What distinguishes Fassi zellige from other types of mosaics are several factors:

1.  **Entirely Manual Technique**: Every piece of zellige is prepared completely by hand. The process begins with kneading the Fassi clay (either white or red), then shaping it into square or rectangular tiles. After drying and firing, the tiles are manually glazed with traditional colors, such as cobalt blue, green, white, and yellow.
2.  **The Cutting Tool (Minqash)**: A "minqash"—a small hammer with a sharp chisel head—is used to cut the glazed square tiles into small pieces (**fetats**) of various geometric shapes (such as squares, stars, triangles, and rhombuses). This process requires immense skill and precision.
3.  **Complex Geometric Patterns**: Fassi zellige is famous for its intricate, interwoven geometric patterns, which follow precise mathematical rules. The most well-known of these are the eight-pointed or twelve-pointed stars, which form magnificent and complex artistic compositions.
4.  **Traditional Colors**: The colors used are natural, derived from materials like cobalt (for blue), copper (for green), and manganese oxide (for black). These colors give Fassi zellige a distinctive luster.

### The Production Process

The creation of Fassi zellige is an art in itself and involves several meticulous stages:

* **Clay and Firing**: The clay is prepared and shaped into tiles.
* **Glazing**: The tiles are coated with a layer of colored glaze.
* **Cutting (Naqsh)**: The "Maalem" (master artisan) cuts the colored tiles into small geometric shapes using the minqash.
* **Assembly (Farsh)**: The small pieces are arranged on the floor or wall, embedded in a plaster-and-lime mixture, to create the final tableau. The assembly is done from the back, as the artisan follows the pattern without seeing the colored front directly.

Fassi zellige is used to decorate the floors and walls of mosques, riads, palaces, and fountains, adding a touch of timeless beauty and splendor that reflects the greatness of traditional Moroccan art.


### The Moroccan caftan is a traditional Moroccan women's costume. It is considered one of the oldest traditional clothes in the world for Moroccans. Its appearance dates back to the twelfth century during the era of the Almohad state in Morocco. It is considered a symbol of nobility, elegance, heritage and identity in Morocco, especially on happy occasions such as weddings, henna and circumcision.
The Moroccan caftan is no longer confined within the borders of Morocco. Rather, it has become the focus of the world's attention, as it has appeared in international fashion shows and been worn by prominent figures on various occasions.
Caftans and Moroccan craftsmanship
The caftan is made by skilled craftsmen, using silk, velvet, and hand embroidery with gold and silver threads, making it a piece of art that represents the Moroccan craftsmanship heritage.
