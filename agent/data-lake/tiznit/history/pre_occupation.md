The pre-occupation history of Tiznit is defined by its role as a major hub of religious authority, tribal life, and resistance in the Souss-Massa region. Before the arrival of the French, the city was not an imperial capital, but a key center of influence.

### Religious and Spiritual Center
* **A Legendary Origin:** The city's founding is rooted in local legend, which attributes its establishment to a holy woman, Lalla Tiznit, who settled there by a spring. This story gave Tiznit a spiritual significance and a reputation as a blessed and peaceful place.
* **Mā al-ʻAynayn:** In the early 20th century, Tiznit became the base for **Mā al-ʻAynayn**, a charismatic religious leader and a key figure in the resistance against French and Spanish expansion. He established a zawiya (a religious school and lodge) in the city, which attracted followers from across the Sahara and the Anti-Atlas mountains.

### A Center of Tribal Autonomy
* **The Souss Region:** The Souss region, where Tiznit is located, was historically semi-autonomous, with its tribes maintaining a high degree of independence from the central Moroccan government (*Makhzen*). Tiznit served as a gathering point for these tribes, who saw it as a neutral and respected location for trade and social gatherings.
* **Strategic Location:** Situated at the crossroads of key trade routes, Tiznit was a vital link between the southern Saharan regions and the Atlantic coast. Its control over these routes gave it significant economic and political power.

### The Reign of Sultan Moulay Hassan I
* **Consolidation of Power:** The period leading up to the French Protectorate was marked by the efforts of Sultan Moulay Hassan I to assert his authority over the rebellious tribes of the south. In 1882, he led a major expedition to the Souss region and was struck by the strategic importance of Tiznit.
* **Construction of the Ramparts:** To consolidate his power and establish a presence in the region, the Sultan ordered the construction of Tiznit's famous ramparts. The building of this fortified city transformed Tiznit into an official outpost of the central government, setting the stage for its future role in both trade and resistance.

In summary, the pre-occupation era of Tiznit was a time of religious piety, tribal autonomy, and strategic importance. It was a city of spiritual significance and a stronghold of local power long before it became a battleground for Moroccan independence.
