### Fez in the time of the Almohads
The Idrisid state is considered the first independent Islamic state in the history of Morocco, which made Fes its capital and a major cultural and civilizational center.
Then the Almoravids took control of the city, as Fez witnessed urban and economic development during the reign of Yusuf ibn Tashfin in the 5th century AH (the first decade of the Gregorian calendar), who worked to unify both banks of the city within a single wall, and contributed to the revival of economic life by building hotels, baths, and mills.
The fortifications of Fez date to the eleventh century period of Almoravid rule (1056-1147). During the Almoravid reign the two separate cities of the Qayrawani and the Andalusi, which faced each other across the Oued Fez river, were united by a single fortification. Under the Almohads (r. 1130-1269), these ramparts were destroyed and then rebuilt. This fortification was completed by the Marinid dynasty (r. 1196-1549) following the founding of the new administrative center, Fez al-Jadid (New Fez) in 1276.
After a nine-month siege, the Almohads took the city in the year (538 AH = 1143 AD), and its development accelerated during the Almohad era in the 12th and 13th centuries AD, as the Almohad mosque became a university and a center for intellectual and religious currents.
The Banu Marin took control of the city after the fall of the Almohad State; this urban, economic, and cultural development of Fez encouraged the Merinids to choose it as the capital of their kingdom.
Despite the transfer of the government capital to Marrakech, the Saadi dynasty distinguished the city of Fes for its enormous achievements, such as the construction of the corridors of the Al-Qarawiyyin mosque and a number of palaces, the restoration of the city walls, and the construction of two large towers on the north and south sides of the city of Fes.
In (1077 AH = 1666 AD), during the reign of Mawla Rashid, the founder of the Alaouite state, Fez remained the capital of the kingdom before his brother Mawla Ismail chose Meknes as the new capital of the kingdom.
In modern history, that is to say from the reign of Moulay Hassan I (1872-1894 AD), modern means began to enter the city, and it was equipped with paved roads, electricity, and telephone lines, and Fez was the capital of the Kingdom of Morocco until 1912 (that is, the period of French occupation which lasted until 1956 when the capital was moved to the city of Rabat.
