Chefchaouen's history is as colorful and layered as its famous blue streets. Spanning more than five centuries, its story is defined by its role as a fortress, a sanctuary, and a bridge between cultures.

### 15th Century: Founding and Sanctuary

The city was founded in **1471** by **Moulay Ali ibn Rachid al-Alami** to serve as a fortress against the Portuguese who were occupying parts of northern Morocco. Its strategic location, nestled in a valley of the Rif Mountains, made it an ideal military stronghold.

The most significant event in its early history was the arrival of a large wave of **Muslim and Jewish refugees** from Andalusia (Spain). Fleeing the Spanish Reconquista and the Inquisition in the late 15th century, they found refuge in Chefchaouen. These communities brought with them their distinct culture, architecture, and traditions, which laid the foundation for the city's unique character. 

### 16th to 19th Centuries: Isolation and Mysticism

For several centuries, Chefchaouen remained a relatively isolated and "holy" city, largely closed to outsiders and foreigners. This period of seclusion helped preserve its unique Andalusian-Rifian culture and traditions. It became a center of Sufi mysticism and intellectual thought, attracting scholars and pilgrims.

### 20th Century: The Spanish Occupation

The city's isolation came to an end with the arrival of the Spanish in **1920**, as Chefchaouen was incorporated into the Spanish Protectorate of northern Morocco. This period, which lasted until Moroccan independence in **1956**, brought both conflict and transformation.

* **The Rif War:** The city was a key location in the Rif War, led by the Berber rebel Abd el-Krim. After a brief period of independence, the Spanish regained control in 1926.
* **Lasting Legacy:** The Spanish occupation left a visible mark on the city, particularly in the architecture of the "new town" outside the medina. More significantly, it opened Chefchaouen to the world, bringing an end to its centuries of isolation and setting the stage for its future as a popular tourist destination.

Today, the city's history is visible everywhere, from the architecture of its medina to the multi-lingual skills of its inhabitants. It is a testament to its enduring legacy as a city of refuge and a crossroad of diverse cultures.
